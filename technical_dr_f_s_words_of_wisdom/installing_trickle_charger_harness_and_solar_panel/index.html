Since these panels only give of low amperage the best thing to do is to use a multi meter on Amps DC. To do this it needs to be in series in the circuit.
Connect positive from the panel (charger) to the positive of the multi meter, and the negative of the multi meter to the positive of the battery.
Don't use this for big currents as most multi meters will only do like max 10 A but for this circuit it's fine.
If you have the multi meter the worng way arround it doesn't matter you just see a minus in the display.
Solar panels and chargers have a diode in them to protect the circuit and to prevent the charger discharging the battery when there is no sun, so any current you see flowing on the display is the amount you battery is being charged.

Those leads are indeed generic. Other than the ring tongue terminals you attach to the battery, it doesn't really have polarity per se ... You can use the resistance measurement capability of your voltmeter to ensure which side of the connector corresponds to the positive (red) connection and then, well, you know by default which is the negative too.

Would be useful to know more about the solar charger (please don't tell me it's just the unregulated output of some solar panel ...), and your voltmeter.

If a simple unregulated output panel, No need make a science project out of it: http://batterytender.com/products/solar.html In particular, check out the "Battery Tender® Solar Controller". Has all the features of a "smart" charger - you provide the solar panel.

On using your voltmeter, there are a couple of things to check to ensure charging:

1. With your charging setup, battery (= load) disconnected - measure the voltage (direct current, "DC") at the point where you would connect to the battery (you will be measuring voltage in parallel to the charging setup).

The polarity of the voltmeter leads should match that the charger leads as the would connect to the battery, that is red or "+" to red, and black or "-"to black.

If your meter reads say, positive 14.n volts, the polarity of the output of the charger should be fine. If it shows negative, e.g. "-14.n" - then what you read somewhere is probably true.

What is the voltage you're reading?

2. Even though you may read a voltage from the charger, to measure whether or not its actually charging, you need to measure the current being supplied. You will be measuring current in series to the charging setup. Can your voltmeter measure current? If so, how much is the maximum it can measure without damage? Hopefully it's fused ... What is the maximum output current rating for your charger setup? (Sorry if this is obvious) The voltmeter's maximum current capability should exceed that of the charger's maximum output.

Do you have another small powersports battery to test this with? I would try what follows, on something other than the stupid expensive SD battery at first.

Place the solar panel such that it should get exposed to the sun.

Assuming the meter is appropriately capable, AND that the polarity of voltage (determined previously) is correct, connect the negative ("-"/black) lead of the charging setup to the battery's negative ("-") post.

DO NOT TOUCH the metal parts of both the meter leads while doing the next step. You don't want to be part of the circuit ...

Set the voltmeter to read Amps or mA DC (as appropriate), carefully connect the negative ("-"/black lead) of the meter (alligator clip lead is useful here ...) to the positive ("+"/red) lead of the charging setup, and touch the positive ("+"/red) lead of meter to the positive ("+") battery post.

What is the current you're reading?
