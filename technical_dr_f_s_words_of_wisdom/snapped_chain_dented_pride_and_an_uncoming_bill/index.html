Superdan

2013-02-18 18:52:00 UTC
Supersprox ... yes, steel outer. For anyone here - regardless the tooth count, metal/alloy type used - and I can't emphasize this enough - always, always, ALWAYS replace chains and both front and rear sprockets as a set. Beyond personal safety, and if for no other reason, to avoid wasting money on premature wear of new components.

Now you're going to have 50 posts on how guys swap individual components in and out, who've never had an "issue". And maybe for balance, we should post up the x-rays of some of the guys where things didn't turn out so well.

As DIDs are not all created equally, brand of chain should have nothing to do with the cause of this failure. Chain model and chain/sprocket condition (plate elongation, roller wear, sprockets' teeth and saddles wear), chain tension, and any mismatch of component state of wear - may have had more than a little something to do with it.

11K - not much ... How many miles on the chain before the rear sprocket swap Weeksy?

For thos who don't know, because of the layout of the swingarm, frame , motor, the chain will get tighter as the swingarm goes thru its motion when more weight is put on to the bike. If you do not adjust your chain at it's tightest point, and you have the chain too tight you will over stress the chain or bearings. I learned the bearings will fall out of races 30 years ago on my yama SR500. I thought I had a flat rear. No Flat. Then I thought my swing arm bearing were shot. I grabbed the rear wheel and shook it side to side to watch the rear wheel bearings fall from grace.

Red rust on my chains = chain game over. Rust on the links will destory the "O" rings in no time. The "O" rings keep the factory lube inside the chain. The key is to keep the "O" rings happy, and the factory lub inside the chain. Moly lub on the output splines will keep the rust off the shaft longer than no lube.
