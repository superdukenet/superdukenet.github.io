Exhaust Noise, Performance & Tambre - Technical Explaina

Gregz

2007-04-11 04:14:00 UTC
Hi Everyone,

There is considerable lack of information on exhaust sound and timbre & much discussion on forums on this topic. I have formulated a paper on the practical and theoretical knowledge that I have gained over many years. I hope this benefits you, and I hope you can comment and expand this topic further. (The practical information is down the bottom).

Timbre Definition:

Note: Due to the forums discussions on noise and more so, on db killers (baffles); I will focus on them to start with.

-=x=-

As there are many effects on performance with baffles, these can be good or bad depending on your tune and engine. Also, they have an effect on sound but we are not exactly sure how. There are two issues to deal with, these are:
1) Exhaust Noise (level)
2) Exhaust Sound (timbre)

Quiet exhausts, usually have the following traits:
{1} high (or fast) flow decreases noise working with;
{2} smooth flow which also decrease noise
{3} expansion chambers for reflection of sound waves

Air separation when it meets the atmosphere (aerodynamics theory) creates noise. That is when preasurised air meets the unpreasurised atmosphere it will create noise. Smaller internal diameter exhausts have a higher flow speed and will generally run quieter. This can be reduced further if it is limited in turbulence.
Hence, a straight cut exhaust tip and a clean non-turbulent exhaust tube will generally have a low noise level.
A larger muffler cannister will allow sound frequency reflection to cancel itself out before being released into the atmosphere. Multi-shape, or varying shape cannisters are effective in more frequency ranges.

Exhaust sound timbre:
{i} Straight flow does not create noise, only the release of the flow out of the pulse out of the exhaust.
{ii} Sound quality is determined by how the pulse meets the air. It is it rippled of clean cut, etc.
{iii} Material of the expansion chamber, where frequency reflection occurs.

Sound quality is obviously decreased by a technically correct muffler and a 'nice' sounding engine can sound not quite as good to us enthusiasts. refering to the above, an exhaust with smooth flow, clean cut exhaust tip and a nice absorbent canister material will sound 'poor' but will certainly be quiet.

-=x=-

db Killers:
The above methods are not used by db killers. db killers are a cheap (low material and manufacturing cost) way that work. db killers do:

[1] decrease the outlet size and thus decrease flow, like closing a door of a room that has the stereo on will make it quieter.
[2] make a clean exit for the flow (straight cut ends)
[3] create another reflection surface creating further reflection

Where db killers are good:
a) 3 or more cylinder engines, especially smaller capacity engines.
These engines have a small exiting pulse at any one time (unlike singles or twins


b) engines tuned for low rpm torque
This is often called back-preasure. The theory is that a small opening creates capacity less flow, but increases flow speed. This is most useful for gas extraction at low rpm, but becomes restrictive at high.

c) exhaust pulse reflection (different to sound wave reflection)
depending on the tune of the engine and the length of the exhaust system. db killers will reflect a pulse back to the engine (ie exhaust valve). If the tuned length is 'correct' you will see a torque gain in a certain rpm range.

The result is that with most engines, you will feel good throttle response with db killers at low rpm. Also, depending on the tune and reflection of exhaust pulses (ie via exhaust length), you may see a side-effect of de/increase in performance with db killers installed.

Quite obviously, they restrict flow, which means that most engines HP will suffer in the higher rpm ranges where max flow is needed. Although, if your engine's flow does not exceed the reduced capacity of the db killers, then there will not be any performance decrease (excluding discussions on pulse reflection tuning).

Relating to the KTM Superduke, this is why we do not see a dramatic decrease in max HP with db killers fitted as opposed to power with them removed from the Akropovic Mufflers. The LC8 does not flow too much more than the capacity of 2 standard db killers can flow.

-=x=-

If you have read this far, and understood at least some of the theory, you should be thinking that with a bit of brains you can make your db killers perform better and sound good without being too loud. After all, isn't that what our aim is (in no particular order):
- Quiet exhaust
- Good performance
- Nice sound

You can achieve this using the db killers as a base, assuming that that they flow with enough capacity that your engine's needs. Relating to the KTM Superduke, the Akropovic db killer and our engine fit this profile quite well without a huge sacrifice.

Quietness:
You want to achieve a reasonably quiet exhaust so we retain the db killer methodology. As the flow is not decreased, we are happy to use the restriction and reflection methodology in this example.

Good performance
On a separate flow topic, which I will not discuss here, flow must be improved by modifying the intake side of the db killers by rounding the ends to create a velocity stack effect. This will improve their maximum flow performance.
See the below link for ways to improve flow by creating one of the designs, which is technically most suitable.

At this point, if you wish to have low noise and improved power, you could fit them without further modifications.

Nice Sound
Finally, we want to create a nice sound without changing the cannister in this example. The next step is to make the airflow to ripple, and combine one flow with another to create sound. (Just drilling holes or cutting has no effect on timbre).

To improve sound quality you need to create turbulance by making cambers. To do this, there must be several holes that are bent to create a cross-flow of gasses. The more turbulence created the more effect will be on timbre, but will decrease max flow to a minor extent.

Conclusion:
You should end up with db killers that look similar to these. They will be reasonably quiet, flow well, and great a nice sound. Note, that due to the turbulence they will be a little louder than standard db killers, but will not be offensive or harsh because they flow well.

-=x=-

Ways to change timbre and performance that I have not discussed above include some of the following.
These in their own right (especially performance) deserve to have their own essay individually.

There are many other ways to modify exhausts to have the similar tambre effects, like:
- rippling the internal tubes
- fitting chevrons to the exit of the exhaust
- creating different size expansion chamber sections
- using different packing materials (can be used with the above modification)
- creating cambered runners, which act like multi chambered cannisters

Flow improvements can be addressed in the following ways:
- creating velocity stack roundings to improve exiting flow
- rounding off the end cap, where the pulse exits (which I have also done on the db killers above)
- creating a gradually expanding chamber, that increased the further down the exhaust
- fit a tuned length reflector (eg. like the White Brothers e-series exhaust)

Remember, performance and noise level is different to timbre, you can have a nice sounding exhaust without it being too loud.
