some good reasons to keep the O2-sensor working

bic_bicknell

2009-12-03 12:39:00 UTC

Hi there!

First of all I like to compliment this forum and its members for this huge amount of information collected here.
For me the engine is the essential, the heart of an motorcycle. Anything making it stronger, enhancing response and its ability to accelerate instantly sharpens the whole character of the bike.

In this way, I guess, the search for the best (engine) performance possible unifies all people here me included.

There are some standards here what to do to improve the KTM V2-engine. One of them is the elimination of the O2-sensor, the lambda probe. Although this mod is so popular, it offers more disadvantages than benefits:

In our days all vehicles are emission-controlled. But fortunately the test-cycle for verifying emissions is rather artificial. It does not simulate real world motorcycle riding but cruising in town from traffic light to traffic light. The highlight of these test procedure is a "speeding" at 120 km/h or 12 kw (=15 HP) engine power.
To pass this emission test it is sufficient to "clean" just these first 15 HP of the engines´s operation range. This means for the KTM only the first three millimeters of the throttle grip´s opening need specific care and control, and this up just to 5250 rpm.

This are also the limits for the closed loop operation of the engine, where the lambda probe controls the engine fueling. When running the engine with more load and/or higher speed it works "open loop" without sensing its lambda probe at all, running exclusively on the internal and programmed map of the ECU, controlled only by "speed density", that means throttle position and engine speed.

The mixture control by the lambda probe during "closed loop" is really subtle, as this sensor itself is a rather subtle thing. A narrow band probe as used in our V2 engine acts as an active sensor, creating - like a small galvanic element or battery - an own and independent current. This type of sensor is responsive to the presence or absence of oxygen within the exhaust gas. If there is no O2 the lambda probe shows its saturation voltage of approx. 1V, if there is plenty of O2 the voltage is zero.

During closed loop o the ECU diversifies the amount of fuel injected to the engine by altering pulse width at the injector. The target for this operation is to keep the mixture within the (famous) lambda window. The voltage, the lambda probe discharges, indicates if the mixture, the ECU´s injector timing, is still within the lambda window or not.
The upper (and rich) limit of that lambda window is reached when the probe senses 0,8V, the lower (and lean) limit is set by an output 0f 0,1V
In closed loop the ECU changes its injected amount of fuel continuously as long as the probe voltage remains between these two limits.

The following pic shows a data recording of the probe voltage during closed loop of KTM- Keihin ECU.



As you can see the "high-voltage"-periods are much longer than the "low-voltage"phases. This shows, that the KTNM ECU holds the mixture in closed loop preferably at the "rich" side of the window. This is a feature of pretty all fuel injection systems for motorcycles. When calibrating an ECU for a car usually the preferred window side is the lean one with the low voltage for better fuel economy, the richer mixture of a motorcycle tunes it for drivability.

In old terms of Carbon monoxide- reading this upper limit of the closed loop window corresponds to 3% CO. That is not so bad for cruising. So if there are problem with drivability during "closed loop" and cruising,the week mixture is not the reason for this.

And there is the Acra-map too, available thru the KTM dealer network. This map does not switch off "closed loop" operation but minimizes its range exclusively to idle. So you may pass an annual emission test without any problems.
In the moment to twist the throttle so far,you can ride the bike, the Acra map is open loop with tons of fuel for the engine.

So in my opinion there is no need to change anything with the lambda operation, because there is no harm with it.

The installation of an O2-eliminator makes things even worse. This eliminator is no piece of high tech. You know - there is a nice DIY-thread here around -there are just two resistors soldered in to simulate the probe.
This Meg-Ohm at the sensor wire indicates only the presence of a probe test cell to the ECU. This avoids the flashing of the check Engine light in the dashboard. But the active and own current a real probe produces, is missing.

This information is confusing for the ECU. The message:"There is a probe attached,but there is no sensor current at all" occurs during normal engine operation only,when the probe is too cold shortly after engine start. In this situation the ECU tries its best to heat up the engine by running a rich mixture.
This would not be so bad. But unfortunately within this extended cold start period the ECU hangs now on by waiting for any sensor voltage, the atmospheric corrections according ambient temperature and pressure for mixture compensation are disabled.

So by installing such a simple O2-eliminator you are reducing the sophisticated intelligence of a modern FI-system to that of an old syle carburetor and make the bike sensible for a different altitudes and weather changes.

The world is not perfect but a sea of possibilities.

I am not talking here about FI from a theoretical point of view, my profession is to make engines perform better an satisfy customer´s needs. So we do not have to argue about the fact, that especially KTMs are in show room condition far away of being perfect regarding throttle response, driveability and engine culture .

The biggest short coming of the KTM V2 is its non-linear throttle response. In stock condition the "carburetion" does not transform slight throttle openings into engine pull clearly and analogically to throttle position: When twisting the throttle grip gently and close to nothing, the engine unfortunately does not respond; when twisting the grip just a little more this creates a sudden rush of power.

This behavior makes the ride really unpleasant. Especially in corners this unintentional "power explosion" is anything but comfortable: In the roll phase after braking before the corner´s apex, when you look, if the engine is still "alive" and start to accelerate by a shy opening of the throttle, such an unexpected power rush is the last thing you are waiting
for.

So many questions, where to start.

I travel around and work on different dynos. The highlight is a full programmable eddy current dyno, discharging its absorbed power into the electricity network, which is fully programmable to simulate a race distance.

But to be honest my favourite dyno is my old Dynojet roller with the serial # 27, I purchased in 1990. This pure inertia drum shows me everything I need to know about a bike.So I feel no need to upgrade it.
I am aware this may sound strange to some of yours, is there the rumour out, only with a brake you can tune in a fuel injected bike. But the background of this claim is not so far technique, I think, it is indeed marketing, to sell the braked and expensive dyno models.
This is no dyno thread but believe me, there are good reasons for me to favourite the simple inertia roller type.

The gas analyzer is a sniffer in the exhaust muffler´s end with a wideband probe on driven by an own curcuit board. So it is fast and accurate and its is possible to sychronize its signal perfectly to the engine pull durung a dyno run.
This is done mainly for "mechanical reasons", because it means a huge effort to weld plugs to each bike to work on. So I analyse just the summary of exhaust gases for all cylinders emerging thru this muffler. But this is no real disadvantage (more later).

The lamda signal of the analyzer now is merged with the injector timing of the ECU, the driving pulses, the engine load as throttle position by the signal of the corresponding (TPS) sensor and engine speed.
This values are the input of my "Kastl" (Austrian slang for "box"):



You may ask: where is the rpm-signal? The ECU injects fuel every 2nd crank rotation. So measuring engine speed means nothing but counting the pulses over time
The "Kastl" uses this input and drives the injectors by its own map according engine speed and load. The basis for this operation is the stock fueling by the ECU, the way it drives the injectors. When there is a need for more fuel, the "Kastl" prolongs the genuine ECU pulse for the injector, if there is to much fuel around, it shortens it. And in the case, the ECU fueling is simply perfect or the ECU works on "closed loop", the "Kastl" changes the pulse not at all and copy it as it is.

The working principle of the "Kastl" is pulse width modulation, nothing revolutionary, the same way as the Dynojet PC, Rapid Bike and other (advanced) "piggy back systems" handle this subject.
Please avoid asking me: "What makes the "Kastl" better?" It makes nothing better compared to its competitors. All this system, and hereby belongs the Aussy TuneEdit too, are just tools to manage fuel. All this system are able to to this job. This systems are just tools to create a new and better matched fuel curve for the engine. So talking about which system is better is pretty the same as discussing if a Keihin main jet is better as a jet manufactured by Mikuni for a carburetor.
At the very end all these "boxes" and devices are "electronic jet kits" and finally the success of the application depends only upon how this "mixture tools" are used.

For me the best thing for the "Kastl" is the fact it is mine, so I know that is going on inside. It is designed firstly as measuring device capturing injectors pulses as accurate as possible. The modulation following is really nothing else but a % plus or a % less for the pulse width pushing the injector.
This "really nothing" sounds so common. But especially in the case of the TuneEdit if have good reason to be in doubt if the input at the screen, the user interface at my lap top, is transferred in the desired way into injector timing.



This is the best part of my "Kastl": its analysis function. The pic shows the injector timing at front and rear cylinder during a run in top gear with a Superduke at my old fashioned inertia roller Dynojet. The white line below is the corresponding Lambda by the wideband sniffer plugged into the muffler ( the bar graphs for "rpm" and "TPS" are deleted here).
This white line shows the fueling needs some help. It is to lean at the start of the dyno run (aprox.3000) and to rich at the very end of the range.



This pictures shows the "after".The green line now shows the modified injector timing, the white line below the corresponding lambda. Now it es flat over the range.
This analysis is optimized for its purpose on an inertia dyno (my favourite tool) with no steady load but only acceleration.

But please let as stop here. It is not my intention to talk here only about fuel injection and mixture problems. I try to lay the focus on another thing: The mixture, the fueling or jetting is just the final, the cap stone of a tuning or engine set up progress. The proper A/F ratio releases the best out of the engine. But if there are other instances within the set up, the periphery of the engine, wrong or mismatched, even a perfect mixture does not cure this short coming, it just helps to mask it.
The KTM V2 is prone to fluctuations of its rotation at low speed. Now if there is any abnormal lash in the drive line to the rear wheel - by a non adequate tensioned and/or worn chain for example - this transforms the bike into a real "kangaroo" no matter how perfect the mixture is controlled. This sounds very plain. But if the "surge" becomes worse I would start an investigation here before trying to adjust the fueling.

In the same way I am pretty sure to have identified "causes" for this surge in other, non ECU-related areas of the motorcycle. And I am willing to share this experience with you. I am here in this forum not to indoctrinate you or sell something but to share my knowledge and learn thereby more engine technique.
But this will be the subject of my next posting.

Yes, I don't believe that intake temeprature and pressure sensors are disabled as a result of bypassing the 02 (Lambda) sensors. What I do beleive occurs is that the ECM defaults to a fixed A/F ratio, and uses mapping for that as a function of these other sensors, throttle inputs, etc.

I often ride from sea level where I live to upwards of 1700 - 1800M elevation, in ambient temperatures ranging from 0 to 45C. No issues w/o 02 sensors. Probably needless to state, the engine doesn't make as much power at elevation (less oxygen), but no change in engine temperatures, throttle response, or engine operating quality ("smothness").

Until only a few years ago, all moto FI systems ran open loop WRT A/F ratio. You tuned with an 02 sensor at the exhaust, picked a ratio, and dyno'd accordingly. That ratio was typically richer than the ideal stoichiomtric ratio (you typically ended up in the range of 12 to 13.5:1).

My slightly older vintage and Suzuki and Ducati (4V) v-twins work this way, and out of the box work well in all conditions. OK - the duc doesn't like to cold start - lol - but its single throttle-plate eliptical throttle body injection is the smoothest I've experienced on a V2. They certainly worked better than my SD did until bypassing the crude 02 sensor (and fueling scheme) that KTM has apparently been forced to use to meet Euro3 requirments.

While requirements for moto emissions (including acoustic) are not as stringent as europe in most other markets, tailoring the system for each specific market isn't the most practical matter. Probably beyond the means of KTM. While a substantial volume motorcycle company, they don't have the resources of the Big 4 and BMW.

My expectations of moto FI off-to-on-throttle transition smoothness are not that of a set of CV carbs, but I can state that forcing the SD to run open loop A/F eliminated most of the offending low speed fueling problems.

I do agree with many of the remarks here regarding the undersized airbox (low speed operation resonances), and understand how just about any improvement in the airbox flow with an effective elimination of these resonant cavity artifacts would result in smoother (particularly lower engine speeed) operation. The tradeoff here being one of sound ("noise" to some) going to a motobox and less restrictive cans (w/o catalysts).

I really would have preferred to have left the 02 sensors alone, as I don't mind a clean running bike. But the SD runs soooooo poorly this way. KTM really didn't do their homework on this one. Preferring it seems, to leave the bike testing to MX/Enduro racers who know only two thottle positions: closed and WFO.

Local dealers (and KTM customer service) are either non-responsive or want to sell you a pile of expensive aftermarket hardware amd services (we know where their interests lie ...). On top of an already expensive bike, this isn't acceptable. I felt compelled to apply the best affordable compromise - and for me - its elimination of the 02 sensors. Obviously, the manipulation (partial elimination) of the 02 sensors' function in alternative exhaust ECM maps, plays a significant role in improvement of SDs' and SDRs' low speed operation.

What I'd like to know is the measured A/F ratio when the 02 sensors are bypassed (eliminated). Anyone measure that (without any change in mapping)?
