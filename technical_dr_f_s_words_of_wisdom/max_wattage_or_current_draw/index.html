Can you give me some info what you want to do?
I can put my amp meter (clamp) over the cable and measure it as it does DC. The issue is that when it's running it also charges, when it's not the coils and injectors don't work.
Not that easy. In general the alternator is calculated to charge the battery in the worst scenario like, all lights on, indicators going one side, cold, just started coils, ecu and injectors going engine not at full rpm.... and so on ... you get the idea....
If the plan is to run an total loss system on your race bike you need to look at the power the ecu, injectors and ignition need so check the fuses as that is a good start to give you an idea what is required maximum.

Sorry man - I have no idea.

Not real practical (or accurate) to put an inductive ammeter on the thing, unless running it under full load ... could use a shunt I suppose (with a very small voltmeter; have several of each handy), and run it around the neighborhood with lights disconnected. Assuming I don't get fined for it ... Sadly, I don't have my own dyno to test this on.

There's no merit in running total loss on a SD IMO ... seriously, if this is a racing issue and you want to be competitive, the amount of weight savings isn't going to get you on the podium, if you're not already on it. Probably another bike more more well suited to the class(es) you race in, is a better approach. You want to win, or racing a SuperDuke for the sake of racing a SuperDuke? Point: If superior bike setup, skill and racecraft don't make up whatever other disparities there are, a few pounds of weight won't make a difference.
