Hi TakisSD,

Backpreasure is a myth of the people without knowledge. The answer is actually tuning, or more specifically resonance formulas for each part of the engine.

By default, almost all the world's engine 'users' get to play with the final product that a manufacturing company 'tunes' or designs. Therefore, we are using designs all of which have a purpose that does not suit max power or performance.

Hence we modify them.... but you are still modifying something you have no formula's for or idea on the exact purpose (and therefore baseline tune). Here lies the dilemma.......

So what is the baseline that we all must start with..... lets think:
- Must pass emissions regulation to be able to sell (exhaust, noise, etc)
- Must be cheap to produce (aka must make $ from sales)
- Must last many years and be durable - minimal warranty claims
- Must be easy to ride (ie. not a GP bike in performance) if people die on your bikes, you will sell less
- The bike must be esthetically pleasing - so they sell more
- etc, etc

All of these factors are money and profit based, and impact on the design and performance of bikes. If 'our' aim is to have a GP bike, then we see most of them as 'detrimental' to our needs.

The problem is that we don't know how the factory has 'tuned' your item and what factors are used unless we blueprint the engine and have all our formula's on hand. Even then, we do not know why some items are made or 'tuned' that way - is it cost, design, requirements, or something else.


So back to the original question.... by design most engines are designed for lower rpm performance and stability. This is where a long/narrow exhaust works best... this in itself may or may not be the determining factor, but it does work and in most cases produces the best result.

The issue is that one of the end results to us lay people is that a long narrow pipe has 'back pressure'. Whatever this means, is a symptom not a cause. In essence, back-pressure is only one variable amongst many and as a single syptom - may improve 'low' rpm performance depending on your tune, and what 'low' means to your application.

As a general rule, what we can measure we can prove. What we measure can be compared. When we compare we can deduce and hypothesise - and hopefully test. In the end measurement is important.
Therefore, find as many write-ups about back-pressure and see what number of measurement they state. Or, talk to people and ask how they measure back-pressure; what tool and measure do they use?
To pre-empt, most if not just about everyone does not measure or doesn't know how to.

In summary, back pressure means nothing, it is a term people throw around to make themselves sound knowledge able.
