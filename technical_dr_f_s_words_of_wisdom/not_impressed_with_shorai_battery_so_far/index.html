Interesting insights - and sorry to learn of the early demise of the Shorai. I hope the vendor takes care of you on this.

You'd think that not unlike same-technology batteries used in say, portable power tools, these batteries would have self-limiting circuitry where you simply couldn't run it flat.

There's also the matter of having balance (equivalence) in the charge of all the cells. Which I think may require a specialized charger - at least, periodically.

Having pretty good luck with Ballistic (http://www.ballisticparts.com/), and they have very solid customer service (at least, over here they do). I have NOT [EDIT] been running them long enough however to state anything definitive about their long term performance = I cannot yet recommend them over the good 'ol sealed Yuasas at this time. Worth noting that it doesn't get freezing (always > 0C) where I'm at; lead-acid if properly maintained can last up to 5 or 6 years in some powersports applications.

Regarding the demise of regs/recs - the design of many are such that they susceptible to being over-driven when a battery is at or near end-of-life. I suspect that this what is happening in a lot the failures on SDs reported here. The main effect on the reg/rec unique LiFePO4 (LFP) is the way they go flat. Compared to lead-acid, the voltage is maintained pretty well throughout discharge (somewhat a function of temperature and load), where it then more or less falls off a cliff (again, comparatively). Downside is probably pretty obvious to you. The upside of that is that makes cutting off the discharge at some non-harmful limit that much easier. LFP is able to charge faster, maintains charge longer (no load), and is more stable thermally. In addition to having higher much higher charge density (and per unit mass) as well as being less toxic.

Anyway - I hope it gets sorted out for you.

Very interesting reading. I had heard about the 'warm up' thing but never thought that 5 minutes might be required! I might just try this tomorrow morning.

The symptoms and experiences outlined in this article are exactly what I'm finding. If the initial crank doesn't work then often the second or third attempt does. And if I put the management system on 'charge' rather than 'store' it only takes about ten minutes to get a better result.

What is, of course, really annoying with the SD is that if the initial crank does not work and fire the engine the clocks show a DAT error code and, worse still, the clock sets to zero. The SD ECU seems to analyse the effect as having a bad battery and lets you know about it. So every time you go out on the bike you have to maybe reset the clock if it bothers you that it shows the wrong time.

I've started to not bother resetting the time, content in the knowledge that the clock now shows me how long ago it was since the bastard shorai battery let me down again.

What I will now try as my new routine is to pop the charger onto 'charge' ten minutes before I want to leave - ie. when I'm putting the bike out of the garage ready for a run and then leave the ignition on for a few minutes before I attempt a start.

Let you know how things turn out using this method.
