Compression Ratio Change - please check my maths

tripoddave

2012-03-03 09:36:00 UTC

It has been suggested that I cut the valves back into their seats by 0.5mm in order to bring the shims needed inside the range of normal KTM Superduke shims.
0.5mm doesn't sound like much but I wanted to see what the effect on the compression ratio was going to be.
I've never done this before so this is working from first principles.

What we know:
Valve sizes:38mm and 34mm
Bore and stroke: 101 x 62.4
Compression ratio: 13:1 (new slugs)

for the purposes of this the symbol we will use for pi (22/7) is: £
therefore swept volume is: £ x 50.5 x 50.5 x 62.4 = 499.94cc

by sinking the valves into the head we increase the volume so we need to work out what that increase is:
£ x 19 x 19 x 0.5
and
£ x 17 x 17 x 0.5
but there's 2 of each valve (2 x 0.5 = 1) so we can loose the 0.5.
our increase in volume looks like this:
(£ x 19 x 19) + (£ x 17 x 17) = which is (near as dammit) 2cc

so if the compression ratio at 499.94cc is 13:1 what decrease in compression do we get if we increase the volume by 2cc?
for clarity what percentage is 2cc of 499.94cc?
(2/499.94) x 100 = 0.4%
a decrease in the compression ratio of 0.4% reduces 13:1 to 12.948

In other words, negligible.
Is this correct?

Ditch the cams.

The lobes are not the right size. If you measure the diameter of the cam lobes (not around the section that provides the lift)you'll see that the diameter is smaller than the stock cams.

Since you've gone to all the other expense get proper cams.

A real down side to what you are describing is the rate at which the aftermarket cams will open and close the valves. Larger diameter lobes (such as the stock cams) will allow the valves to be opened and closed at a gentler pace prolonging valve/valve seat life.

Just a thought...

If the displacement is 500cc this means:
(500cc + head volume) / 13 = head volume

500cc + head volume = 13 x head volume

Head volume = 500cc / 12

Take this head volume, add 2cc and check the compression ratio again.
Compare results.

Note: thee calculations are adiabatic so this is just a guideline.

Best thing is to check compression after rebuild and if needed increase the compression using a thinner gasket.

With a compression ratio of 13.1 with the current setup, increasing the head chamber with 2cc will result in a compression ratio of 12.54
To get back to the 13.1 compression ratio you'll need to lower the cylinder head 0.25mm compared to the crankcase by aether using a thinner cylinder base gasket or sand/flatten a quarter mm of the head base.

Calculations:

Current head volume:
500cc / ( 13.1 - 1 ) = 41.32cc

Increase with 2cc makes it 43.32cc leading to a compression ratio of
( 500cc + 43.32cc ) / 43.32cc = 12.54

Lower the head to make up for the 2cc ( = 2000mm^3 )
2000mm^3 / ( 3.14 * 101mm * 101mm / 4 ) = 0.25mm

Since we are at the slagging off stage I can add that increasing the compression on the SD (or any similar V-twin) by using a thinner gasket or machining the heads is a NONO!
The reason is that the timing chain ends up too long and the tensioner will pull the chain tight in one direction upsetting the cam timing for starters.
To increase the compression properly it needs other pistons or adjustable cams-sprockets.

I didn't say anything about lift or duration. You can go as far as you want.....within reason.

When you check your valve clearance, you place a feeler gage between the bucket and the cylindrical portion of the cam lobe..not the portion of the lobe that actually moves the valves. The smaller the diameter of this cylindrical portion, the thicker the shims have to be to get proper valve adjustment. This also leads to excessive acceleration of the valves in both directions. You want an aftermarket cam that has the same dimension on the cylindrical portion of the lobe as the stock cam. You can go nuts with the lift and duration measurements if you so desire....depends on what kind of power you are after.

Without a drawing you may not be able to understand my point. I'll try to post one up in April when I get home and have access to a CAD program.

Good luck with your build.

BTW, the stock comression ratio is 11.0:1, not 10.0:1
