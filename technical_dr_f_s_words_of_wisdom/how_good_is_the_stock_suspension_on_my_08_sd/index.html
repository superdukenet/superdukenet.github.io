From the spec sheet the rear spring is only good for a max 85kg rider, I can confirm that as I weight about that and I had to turn it all the way up.
(no I'm not a hard suspension lover)
The forks seem to be way to hard compared to the back as the bike comes out of the box. The stealer should give some advise about that but few do.
So yes your suspension could do with a overhaul if you're either under or over this weight spec or use the bike extensively for track days or racing.
I found the WP suspension of good quality as standard and working very well if dialed in properly but as mentioned it is often overlooked and if you don't know what to look for or never tweaked it before seek help either from a professional or from an experienced friend.
Don't let them set it up following their tase however as this could be so wrong for you.

I took my bike to HM Racing to get my suspension set up. Perry Leask who runs the suspension unit (a bit of a race guru) reckoned that the WP suspension was "pretty good" for OE equipment and although he changed the front springs to suit my 75kg and changed to a better fork oil he didn't think it was worth upgrading to the only make he thought genuinely better; Ohlins, for road ridng and a few trackdays a year.

I agree that getting your suspension set-up for your weight and riding style is an essential mod (which might need the right springs or changes in oil, etc), and TBH I think dealers should do it for you when you buy a new bike, for free... but, it seems that most pros rate the WP as pretty good OE equioment, and certainly better than OE on just about any other bike including the Jap four and the Euro competition. ...unless your bike comes with Ohlins, you've got good kit already.


...Finally, Perry did say specifically that he though my 08 SDR was sprung way too hard, not just for road use, but for track use too. I would have needed to be at least 85 kilos (dry) for the fork springs to be set up for me. ...and "Hard" is not necessarily good for tracks, as you want the tyre to track every ripple to maintain a contact patch.

The rear sping was ok for my weight, but the oil sits high (apparently) in the stroke, so he had to jack up the rear (even for my 75kg) to make sure the spring was doing the initial damping, not the oil.

[edit - 200lb is 90kg, so your springs are probably good on the 08 SDR]

Standard suspension is always a compromise (even the WP units we have) as it has to cover a huge variety of diffrent riding styles and rider weights, sure re-sprung/valved units will handle better in the right riders hands, the question you have to ask is "are you that good that your going to notice the diffrence"?

My betting is 99% of the guys on here wouldnt notice (and i am including myself in that statement)!

The most down to earth suspension reccommendations i have seen for the standard 07/08 SD is to set your sags correctly, then use the KTM road settings out of the manual for road work and when hitting the track simply take 4 clicks out of both front and rear Rebound and Compression settings and leave high speed compression set at 1.5 turns.

first things first, check your tyres and pressure, once you're happy they're right set the sag, static and loaded, you should be able to set the front without any mods, the rear is a little harder due to the fact they're a two seater, the spring and shock set up is a compromise. If you can get both sag settings right you won't need a new spring. once you have this done make sure the compression/rebound settings are on the std settings, the rear has hi and low speed damping remember. RIDE IT! TRY IT, on the road, preferably a piece of road you know, a few times, think about how the bike rides, feels turns etc. setting the sag correctly is the base line for all suspension settings, if you can't get these right you're wasting your time and money doing anything else, correct sag also offers the biggest cheapest improvement for regular riders. from there you can adjust the compression and rebound depending on how the bike behaves, small increments, keep notes and try it if it's better keep going in small increments if it's worse put it back. there's plenty of places you can find instructions on what does what and how each function and adjustment will affect how the bike rides, and it's a great way to learn more about your bike and what you like which will translate to easier set up in future.

It turned out that the dealer set the front compression dampening WAY too high. I dialed that out, and the bike started to behave. Then I had the sag set and the suspension set up properly. That took care of the rest of the problems, like pogoing, but now the bike wanted to stay leaned over after finishing a turn.

I called the tuner up and he told me to dial one turn of preload out. Now, I'm in bliss. It was an interesting road to get the SD to where it is now. If you know nothing, like I do, go to someone who knows suspensions and have them set it up for you. It will likely be the best $40-50 you've ever spent.

The purpose of suspension is to keep the tyres in contact with the road / track. Thats it, nothing else.

I think al lot of the suspension myth comes from a time when standard suspension was crap and aftermarket units set up well did make a substantial difference. The rest comes from watching professional racers looking for that 0.1% improvement that might give them the edge in a close fought contest.

My point is a simple one, you would be hard pressed to find a good sports bike from the last five or more years where the suspension is a limiting factor for more than a tiny fraction at the very pointy end of the fast group. I spent some years dragging bits of various R1’s, Fireblades, Gixers and others as part of the fast group round many of the UK’s tracks and the only thing holding me back was lack of talent. No amount of upmarket suspension bling would have made any difference at all.

For the road I like my set up as soft as possible without inducing ground clearance, dive, wobble problems and I firm things up for the track as there are usually less bumps and the breaking forces are higher. I’m not intending to do much on the track with the SD but if I did I’m certain the standard suspension would be well up to the job.

If you want to go faster on track invest in a good race school, will make much more difference than any amount of bling. Most of the tuning needs to be done between the ears, that was my experience anyway.

Now my two cents..or pence...
At your weight, you need a stiffer spring on the shock, absolutely. With the stock spring you will need to sacrifice sag for preload. I used the Racetech supplied Eibach 17.8 kg. The stock spring is 17.0. My bike is substantially lighter than stock and I probably could have used the next one up, which is quite a bit stiffer at 19.6 but that seemed too much of a jump for me. There have been cheaper and a better range of spring brands discussed here but I don't recall at the moment.
If you take the shock off, Race Tech makes a Schrader type bleeder that replaces the WP proprietary bleed device. I recommend swapping that out while you are there. Your tech will thanks you in the future.

The fork on my bike feel outstanding. I raced and tracked the bike for two years (YIKES!) before I had them looked at. I just had the bushings replaced and the fluid changed, as well as some other Race Tech School recommended tweaks and I don't feel that they are any different than they were when the bike was new.
As you have probably seen from other posts, I have the Nichols triple and shock clevis.
These are a huge improvement and mandatory, IMO!
Someone suggested getting your geometry right first. These parts do just that.
Worth every penny!
Now, about set up. You really need a competent suspension tuner. The issue is that your bike will never be "set up" as long as you improve your skills/times.
Just when the bike feels great, you gain confidence and shave a couple of seconds off your best lap times. The problem is that you are now in need of a tweak to suspension settings at that pace.
Sort it out and now more confidence.....drop two seconds and there you are again in need of some tweaks.
You get the idea.
This is exactly where I was last year as I got faster each time I hit the track.
It cracks me up when guys say that they have their bikes dialed in. For what pace? If you are at the top of your game and can't improve, you will still find a little tweaking helps.

This year there is a chance that I will have Mike Canfield dialing me in this season. If you recall, he was Martin Cardenas' suspension tuner last year. He ended up with a #1 plate in AMA Daytona Sport Bike.

The SD has an amazing combination of suspension and brakes, right out of the box.
Most guys I know that have R1s, etc, spend a shitload of $$$ right away on Ohlins components. Simply not needed until it is needed for the SD, IMO.
I would suggest Vesrah SRJL brake pads. They were very instrumental in my dropping lap times. Expensive but INCREDIBLE!
