Don't rely on an ohm meter to test the starter dude. The windings are so big in a starter engine that the measure will always indicate 0 ohm.

Since you disconnected the starter try it on a battery from a car with jumper leads.
It is the only way to try it with limited tools to make sure it is fine.
In my opinion the problem is somewhere else like a faulty wire or negative connection.
Follow the wiring from the battery all the way to the starter and check for broken connectors, pay attention to heat shrinked bits as the copper eyelet might have broken under the shrink.

To try the starter, disconnect the wire that goes to the starter motor from the starter relay and hook your positive jumper lead to that, this will prevent the connector on the starter to get damaged (tread), make sure it doesn't touch any other metal part of the bike! hook the negative to a solid aluminium point on the engine near the starter or the negative bolt cable on the frame. connect the negative to the battery and touch the positive jumper solidly to the positive of the battery without clamping it on. It will spark and be ready to pull back if the starter is really fooked. it will not turn over if so, be ready to get it off before it welds on.

PM me if you have more questions!

you should check continuity of the main leads before attempting to jump the starter, if there is a break under the heat shrink on the pos. lead your efforts will be fruitless and you may misdiagnose your problem. use your meter on ohms, test the test leads together, you should get a beep indicating continuity, check each lead, if they all check out ok then jump the starter as ktmguy describes. if that checks out also you should check volts across your battery while you hit the starter button, even new batteries can be stuffed, i've recently seen a cbr600 that had a healthy 13v at the terminals with the ignition off, 12.? with the ign on and nothing, 0, dead short with the starter button on, and it was a new battery.
As a rule, starters don't just stop working, they tend to slow up as the brushes and contacts wear out, this is usually because we start the bike with poor charge in a battery, i can't reccomend it enough, get a battery tender, they save more than just the battery!

good luck.

Hello .. I hope you're up and running by the time you read this, but if you still have a problem. Two years ago I had the same problem you have described. My problem turned out to be faulty NEW battery. You said that the NEW battery voltage was good. What was the reading ? With no load the battery should to be over 13 volts. You can have a battery read in the low 12 volt range and only be at 40% capacity( 40% will not crank a SD ) I don't know your local situation, but i would have the shop where you purchased the battery run a load test on the NEW battery. ( Note: Auto shops with electronic battery testers can also test bike batteries. )

Good luck with this, Kuerbis
