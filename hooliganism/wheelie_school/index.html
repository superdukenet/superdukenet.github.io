Hi cjc,
The school is expensive and you more than likely will not get to the balance point. Alternatives are: friend with dirt bikes who will take you out and let you have at it, buy a cheap 200, 250 four stroke and put a larger rear sprocket on then get in the dirt and have at it. Never practice alone and wear your gear. Also, cover the rear brake. If you just have to do it on the SD, 2nd gear is good. Not as jumpy as first. You don't even need to use the clutch. Just roll to about 25 - 30mph then roll the throttle on hard. It'll take you a few tries but you'll start to find where it wants to come up and where it doesn't.

Relax, move back if you need to and have fun!

Best of luck,
Chuck.



btw, I'm not trying to tell you not to do the school. I'm just presenting other options that, for the money, might give you more opportunities to learn.

Hey Gary,
If you can, have a friend video tape you. Sometimes it's just a matter of being able to see what you're really doing so when you do it right, you can equate that with feeling it.

Quick example: I did a trackday with a stock EX 250. I skipped a couple of B group sessions to sit in with the C group instructors during their class. I learned a few things on body position and went out and practiced it. The difference was night and day.

It really sunk in when I saw what I was doing because before that, I could have sworn I was in good position.

before with bad position


after with much better position


Now that I know what the correct way feels like, I can repeat it but I wouldn't have known without seeing for myself that I was in a bad position.

Hope that helps and best of luck. Oh yeah, post pics!
Chuck.

2007-12-21 09:27:00 UTC
Yeah, wheelies are great fun, look fantastic and make you feel like a riding god.....but do increase your chances of a crash, specially when learning or trying new techniques.

I flipped mine over backwards changing up from 2nd to 3rd on the balancing point......I now know to change up gear on the way up, not when you've already made it to the top!

Clutching up in first gear sounds like a recipe for disaster to me, unnecessary too.

You'll also find the bike is quite sensitive to the amount of fuel you're carrying, be aware that the front comes up A LOT more easily when you have a light fuel load, it can be a bit of a pig with a full tank.

I too have booked a wheelie school for the Spring. Myself and four others from another forum I frequent are doing Jimmy Fireblade's school of the clutch method in Lincolnshire in May.

From experience of being able to lift off the throttle small wheelies (nowhere near the balance point) I would suggest the following:

1. DO NOT under any circumstances try to perfect the power method in 1st gear on a SD. Its far too powerful and does not allow smoothness.

2. 2nd gear lifts very nicely at about 25-30 mph with a flick of the wrist (throttle) and a very slight tug on the bars.

3. I have a distinct feeling that the amount of revs required to clutch up a 4 cylinder bike (we will learn on 600 Bandits with an ignition cutting "wand" on the back) in 2nd gear will be far too much on the SD which by the feel of things thus far would only require low revs if clutching up....

Any more advice appreciated. The putfile video don't work at work here.
