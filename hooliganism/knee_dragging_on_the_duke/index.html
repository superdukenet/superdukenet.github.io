You have learnt your first track lesson: kneedown is not the fastest way.

Well, not if you are reaching out to the ground with your knee as many people do.

Watch a racer, preferably a 125 racer, and you will see he doesnt stick his knee out he pushes it forward to weight the front of the bike.

His knee may touch down. Generally this is because his lean angle is great enough due to the speed he is carrying. Or the ground is rising toward him - adverse camber.

There are plenty of people who can drag a knee with the bike almost upright road or track. And there are plenty of track riders that waste time hanging a knee out as soon as they brake for the corner.

Top racers use their knee as a lean angle gauge but they can only do this if they keep the knee reasonably close to the side of the bike. They can also save the front this way. You or I cant as we dont have their cat like reflexes!

The rest of us lean until we get a toe slider down. Then, if the corner continues wait until ourr knee brushes the ground and use this as a signal to start standing the bike back up.

Usually, when my knee scrapes the tarmac these days it is my left knee. I used to think this meant I leant further on my left side. It probibly means I am lazy correcting the steering on the left hence slower getting back on the throttle.

Hi Lobster

All of my kneedowns have been on track. I have never tried on the road. Usually you would need a long wide sweeper or a roundabout or somewhere that you know you can use both sides of the road into and out of the corner.

The trouble on roundabouts, for me, is vision. I just wouldnt feel comfortable with my head down on one side of the bike going round with the chance of cars emerging unseen.

What I have noticed is that the magazine testers use a totally different technique on the road. Last month I followed Michael Neeves around Brands and he was riding like a racer. - Half his ar*e off the seat, knee down and forward and good lean angle and corner speed.

When you see him in MCN testing road bikes he is hanging off like a gibbon. The bike is almost upright, there is a big V between his upper and lower leg and his ar*e is totally out the seat.

No critisism of Neevsie he gets paid to ride like that. but I think most of the info out there is based on carrying corner speed to bring the bike over enough and this is not always possible on the road.

If your tyre edge is cooked it sounds like you have enough lean angle. Next time you are out place your feet right on the outside edge of the footpegs. If your toesliders touch down on roundabouts / corners you definately have enough lean.

The rest can only be body position. If you are comfortable with it, try the exaggerated position you see in bike mags. But remember, its main purpose is to look good in photos not keep good control of the bike.

Otherwise, do a couple of trackdays - you will definately do it there. Best of luck.

Look, I'm an old guy who can't ride, but I'll tell you something that applies not only to the Duke but to all bikes in general: you either ride the bike the way it wants to be ridden or the way you want to ride it. Riding with the intention to drag your knee because it looks cool or makes you feel like a badass instead of riding the bike with proper entry, trail braking, apex and accelleration may not yield optimal results.

The Duke, owing to its higher riding position , makes it harder for the knee dragging thing. This weekend I was ripping up a back road; in one sweeper I moved farther forward on the bike and hung of the side farther than I had been doing.

Once I started listening to the bike and not to my ego, things resumed a great ride. Moral of the story: kneedraggging looks cool but is unneccessary, and may be dangerous on a tall bike like this. If you want to drag your knees, maybe a GSXR or Ducati is what you need.

The whole objective is to get as much of the riders weight as possible low and to the inside of the tyres contact patch, so the bike doesn't have to be leaned over as much. You have more tyre in contact with the road, and better traction. The whole knee down thing isn't really necessary, it certainly doesn't stabilize you any better or anything like that. What is does is provide a gauge for the experienced racer to know how much more angle he has left in a corner. You never need to do this on the road- but yes it is fun. For those that cant do it, you need to keep your weight low over the tank, slide one cheek entirely off the bike, and keep your weight on the inside peg. Try to grip as much of the tank as possible with your outside knee and upper leg. Keep on the balls of your feet, and keep a light grip on the bars. You'll get it in no time.
