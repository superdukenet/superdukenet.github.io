I am also of the view that correct front springs for a rider's weight are of value here - not just cranking up front preload to get sag more or less correct. I am no scientist. Well, actually I am technically, but I don't have the facts or figures about rake, trail, etc. on the SD nor the SDR.
Since all of the development on the triple was done for GoGo on the SDR I would have to assume that the front needed to be brought up by those 20mm.
When TriValley brought me on their team, the deal was that I would run the custom parts on my bike too. This wasn't to pimp the Nichols stuff. It was to assist in my success for the racing season. Although the change in geometry was so significant that I had to relearn the bike, the stability was immediately palpable. This combined with my going to the same tyres as the rest of the team's bikes: 120/60 V slick front and the 190/50 DOT rear. Michelin Power One
I absolutely loved the tyres and the way the bike felt once the suspension was dialed in.
It should also be noted that I did nothing to the suspension except swap out for a the 180 spring in the rear. I raced at 190 lbs (80 kgs+/-) and the stiffer spring was necessary to avoid sacrificing sag. With the stock spring I had to crank up the preload because I was pushing the front in sweepers. This was caused by my bike "squatting" and not forcing the front into the ground. The stiffer spring nailed it.

I started the season doing 1:59s at Infineon and ended with 1:49s and complete confidence.

I cannot comment on sliding too much as I just started doing it with both tyres during my last race weekend. I had been sliding the rear everywhere before but only due to my greedy throttle. It was always manageable with my Michelins.
I do remember that in a right hand sweeper that followed the back straight at Las Vegas, other guys would break and slow quite a bit whilst I would just touch the brake for steering and crank the throttle. I would describe it as "sling shotting" around those guys because of my ability to stay on the throttle. The sliding was a bit strange at first but became second nature almost immediately.
I wouldn't agree that sliding slows your times any more than braking through a turn slows you down.
It all adds up to entrance and exits.
I see no point of any track day rider getting this insane about riding, personally.
I only did it because of the incredible competition in our clubs out here.
In the AFM, even pro's who come here to poach very often get beaten.

Man, I miss it all just too much!

If you're sliding already at a slower pace then you're body positioning/english is wrong. You're upper body should be constantly moving and floating around the bike while your legs are locked in with one ass cheek off. You must be relaxed, letting the bike flow where it wants which should be on line with the appropriate entry line and inputs. When the bike starts sliding it's hard to relax. You tense up, the bike slides more. You slow down, you go home feeling bad, or you continue pushing and you crash!

Relax, breathe, flow. Exaggerate your upper body. Avatar>
