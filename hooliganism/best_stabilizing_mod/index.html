SDNerd

2012-06-02 15:01:00 UTC

Ridden my SD at 140+ (mph, about all she'll do in top gear) - was stone stable without a steering damper.

Is this your first bike? Is your SD new?

What some people perceive as insatiability, isn't. Often what's being experienced is an artifact of something else (e.g. death-gripping as suggested, or even something as simple as incorrect tyre pressures).

Maybe if you described in more detail what you're experiencing and what the conditions are when this 'instability' occurs.

As for the suspension setup by a pro, and damper: Start with the suspension. Will be worth every penny. If you want to quell some headshake on WFO low-gear use, and add some damping (stability) to your banzai corner entries while trailbraking - then sure, a steering damper is a nice addition. For most, a steering damper on the SD (not SDR) is gratuitous bling.

Duc sportbikes are well known for what people describe as their turning "stability" - and that's because they tend to use pretty conservative geometry on their bike designs. For some, that's a confidence inspiring feeling. For others, it requires more effort to turn-in, isn't as "flickable", and isn't as easy to put wherever you want just by thinking about it. The S1000RR, isn't exactly radical in this department either. About as close to a Japanese I4 Superbike as europe has ever produced IMO.

Search the forum some, you will learn what others have done to inject more trail on their SDs.

TLS_Russ

2012-06-03 09:01:00 UTC

first make sure that your riding is correct

but you should check that all the suspension items are set up correctly, fork oil height, all clickers correctly set (rebound, compression), all bolts torqued up correctly.

one of the other things that can make a bike unstable in the front is if you dont have enough compression dampening happening in the rear (bike will wallow and cause the bars to shake).

last of all, if you are still having problems, go to a chassis specialist and have them make sure that the chassis is holding the correct geometry.

after being hit by a car, my bike kept on trying to kill me.. turns out that the chassis was twisted pushed in 10mm and 5mm to the left. Chassis straightened out and the bike is a dream again.

in fact, if you take it to someone good, they can make it better than factory (tighter tolerances)
