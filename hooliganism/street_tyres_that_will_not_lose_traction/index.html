The first heat cycle of a new tyre is the most critical,
The latest motorcycle tyres are built with highly sophisticated tread compounds. These compounds are capable of sustaining grip throughout a much wider temperature range, but are also extremely sensitive to the first heat cycle of its use. During this cycle, the tread compound stretches as it heats, breaking the weaker, shorter molecular bonds within the rubber. The benefit of this process is a tread compound that lasts longer and provides better grip. However, if the first cycle is not performed correctly, the tread may develop irregular compounding, leading to poor wear and inconsistent grip.
Rubber is all about grip, and grip related to elastic hysteresis. Have you heard about that

I would like to add my two penny worth if I may?

For road use I would recommend either the Dunlop Roadsmart 2 or Michelin Pilot Road 3 with my personal preference being the Dunlop as I'm British.

For those people who think that they ride too fast for these tyres: you don't. This isn't to say that there aren't some very quick riders on this forum and its not to put down the 'best of the rest'. The point is sports touring tyres actually offer more grip most of the time than their stickier brethren simply because they reach their operating temperature very quickly and stay there. More sports orientated tyres offer more grip when within their operating temperature range but this is much narrower than your sports-touring tyre.

When you get people saying their tyres let go for no reason the most common 'fault' is the tyre is either outside its operating temperature or (especially if using trackday tyres on the road) because the compound has gone off. For example people running Michelin Power 1 often experience them letting go even after a fast run followed by a brief stop because they can drop out of operating temperature range very quickly especially when the tarmac itself is cool.

The other down side to running a sports/trackday tyre is the profile. Track orientated fronts have a more triangular profile allowing them to turn in more quickly and offering a larger contact patch at full lean. The downside to this is at anything less than full lean then they offer a smaller contact patch than their street orientated cousins.

One final thing. For those that don't know me: I am fast closing on 50, short and hugely overweight but I can get me knee down on my GTR1400 on the road using Roadsmarts whilst getting about 7,000 miles out of a set so I know they're good enough!
