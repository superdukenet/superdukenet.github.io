Zenbiker

2011-04-10 13:49:00 UTC
Use drain cleaner to remove the red anodised coating; keep a close eye on them because the process is slow at first then goes fast. Polish the adjusters and degrease them in acetone. I got my sulphuric acid at the auto parts store - battery acid. Mix acid into water at the recommended ratio. I used aluminium MIG welding wire to suspend the pieces in the acid bath. Attach the adjusters and the cathode (I used a piece of 6061 aluminium), and hook it up to a battery charger; start at about 2 amps; too much amperage will result in pitting. It will take approximately 45-60 minutes to anodize the adjusters; you'll know they're done when they take on a slightly yellow grey matte finish. Rinse in HOT water and immediately place in the hot dye until you get to the color intensity you're after. I don't know about boiling them to seal the surface, but it can't hurt. If you don't pit the pieces by leaving them in the drain cleaner too long, it'll take a couple of hours.

2011-04-10 23:36:00 UTC
This is the guide I used and seemed to work all right





I got some scrap overhead power line from the local electricity supplier to use as an anode and make sure you use a fresh piece everytime as the anode also anodises and no longer conducts electricity ( can be cleaned off though). Dye should not be hotter than 50C and the item should then be boiled in distilled water for 30 mins.

Plain anodising without dye also looks good and stops corrosion and hardens the surface of items. I did my heel guards probably 8 months ago and the are still unmarked.



Most important thing is polish the shit out of it as anodising will not hide scratches or marks and make sure its CLEAN.

I use a little esky (cooler) to store the acid in and have another one with a mix of bicarb of soda and water as an emergency neutraliser.

Zenbikers original post put me onto it, its relatively easy and good fun, experiment with some scrap before launching into the bike though.
