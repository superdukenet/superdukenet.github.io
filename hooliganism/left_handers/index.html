This has now become an ingrained problem!

The only way to rid yourself of this fear is too work hard at lefts and become confident.

Start off on the side stand or paddock stand, spend as long as you need and look/compare your body position on rights/lefts, look for the subtle diffirences that may be upsetting your confidence on lefts, get a mate to follow you with a camera on the bike and again compare rights/lefts.

Youl more than likely find your pushing the bike from under you on lefts (kinda like a motorcross style).

If you study your lefts carefully youl probly also notice your getting very tense/nervous and gripping the bars too much and this will cause problems. You may also notice that on lefts you dont look far enough ahead down the road (but rights you do as theres no nerves getting in the way)

These are all things to look at closely.
Finally get yourself along to Rockingham or Bedford, go out in the novice group and only ride at around %80 of your maximum, this will give you time to look at all the above and sort the problems, im sure that by the end of a day at Rockingham you will have sorted whatever it is thats screwing your lefts

Most Roads have a raised crown ,the middle is several inches higher than either side to promote drainage.
If you drive on the right- most right hand turns have positive camber.
If you drive on the left- most left hand turns have positive camber.
Counter steering is usually easier to do to initiate a left turn since your right hand is busier on turn in with throttle and brake inputs. ( I know, not your problem)
What might work to help with this.
On a road or track you are familiar with. Pick out turn in points. When you get there, turn. Counter steer into the turn with your focus on the apex. By the time you get to the apex your focus should have already shifted to the exit.
Braking, Downshifts, turn-in inputs need to done smoothly ,FIRST, with no noticeable change in chassis position.
As you gain confidence you can brake later, use more force countersteering in, and transition to the throttle earlier.
You still need to be smooth.
Smooth is fast.
Fast is Smooth.

Check out your bike if nothing is wrong like the rear axle-chain adjusters way out so the wheels are not aligned, and while your there check tyres and so on. Do some reading on suspension setup and see if it is in the ball park for your weight.
Just do all these checks to boost your confidence nothing is wrong. If you can try on another bike to see if it makes any difference.

Go for a ride and self check your movements and position on the bike including your vision and head position and compare in left and right handers.
If you do something wrong you be able to pinpoint it and correct. If you can't fault yourself or the bike just mentally go for it and it will work.

If you can't work it out do a trackday with an instructor (like california superbike school or so) who can point you in the right direction. It will be worth it as it will improve you abilities and safety on the road and track.
