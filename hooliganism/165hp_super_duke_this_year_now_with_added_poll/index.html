Being in the business of product development and, more crucially, marketing I have to put in my opinion here in response to the posts on this thread - which I find a bit hysterical really.

What we all know about KTM is that they have two distinct values which are very important to them. One is that they are true motorcycle enthusiasts and their bikes are well thought out, designed by people who think about what 'riders' want. OK this comes out as "ready to race" which, (as has been pointed out here), is a bit of a marketing slogan and not absolutely true - but it does capture the essence in which they try and develop their bikes. The other important factor is that they are a small, Austrian company who have had to compete against the huge resources of the big Japanese four, the Italian lot, Triumph and that German company. They've done really well in the last decade because they've come up with some gems of bikes, (from Supermotos, adventure bikes, and now learner bikes, not to mention their core skill around off road machines. Paris Dakar anyone?), that deliver and appeal to a small but growing number of discerning customers.

You guys, are all part of this small, but appreciative group of people who are already KTM converts. Me too. I'll always be one of them - and am proud to be loyal to a company I spotted and started supporting a long, long time ago.

But when you are a growing company that has ambitions to be bigger and a global force things change. You have to get more money and maybe get some backing - so they join forces with an Indian giant who can inject more capital and potential into the business. You start to look for other ways to grow your business - and that includes the learner market, the massive opportunities in commuter bikes, and crucially appealing to the masses - not just guys like us.

And when you're a marketing led company, (which they undoubtably now are), they have to ask people what they want. And that means market research, testing, listening to the potential target market. Trying out ideas and concepts and showing people what they might do. OK they're not asking me and you direct. We are too small a niche group. It's not that they don't care, they know we are fans, they know we will be looking at their next product whatever they do. They want to connect with the thousands and thousands of people out there who DON'T know what a KTM is. Who DON'T want a hard styled, all or nothing bike, who want an exciting, but maybe less challenging concept of a bike to own.

Lets face it. The majority of bikers and potential customers out there are NOT YOU GUYS!

So, in my opinion the next SD will be everything a KTM should be. They will give the market what they need to do to be successful. As you guys have pointed out, they know what they are up against. They will deliver a package that has a competitive BHP output. They will ensure it handles brilliantly on road and track because that is what they know it will be judged against. They will make sure that it generates rave reviews however it is tested - they have an excellent track record of achieving this - don't doubt them now.

But it will also be styled and executed in a way that is designed to appeal to a much broader market. It will, maybe, appear to be 'less hard'. It may be less uncompromised. It may look less aggressive and have a few things which we all feel make it a 'softer, dumbed down option". But this will be because it was designed to sell to a broader market.

And we can get hold of it and do our thing to it. Strip off the SAS, junk the huge silencers and CAT. Fit race rear sets and take off the safety features and ABS. etc. etc. MAKE IT LOOK MORE AGRESSIVE. Change the ride height. Isn't this what we've always done to our bikes anyway. fook, in the 70's the bikes that came out of Japan were NOWHERE near what we wanted. You had to change EVERYTHING.

You guys seem to want everything done for you.

This bike will be a HUGE advancement on the current design we all ride and love. It will be a BETTER BIKE. KTM have NEVER designed a shit bike to my knowledge. But they could easily design bike that was aimed at a mass market and looked shit, (started with the Duke and LC4 already). But I've been converting them for over thirty years in to the bikes I really want to own.

I just intend to continue to do so.
