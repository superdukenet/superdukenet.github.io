leatt also makes similar products and seem to be getting more press than astars. seems like a good idea, but somethings to consider:

1. (obviously) no product can guarantee against deadly/debilitating neck injury from an accident
2. your fusion means you won't have full range of motion, period. all of these braces are meant to be adjusted for somebody with a "normal" neck to stop it from exceeding the safer limits. Your brace, in theory, will need to be adjusted so it limits much earlier in your range to lessen the risk of damage to your fusion. if you discussed this w/ your doc, perhaps consult with him further on what movements they'd like you to restrict most per the procedure u had

delving beyond the gear w/o derailing this post, why the fusion in the first place? was it for painful symptoms? did the fusion succeed in this regard or did it not, leading to the radio ablation? did you try physio before to address the pain? (sorry i'm a PT and see plenty of folks w/ neck pain who've had fusions w/ limited improvement. feel free to NOT indulge me)

have fun getting back to riding, stay safe!

I read an article in Motorcyclist today about the Leatt STX Brace. They gave it high marks for comfort and range of motion. The only criticism they had of the brace was that it choked them when in a full tuck.

"At speed, you don't notice the low-profile STX at all. Most helmets only contact the brace at the limits of normal motion, but check compatibility with your preferred lid. Shoei, Araai and Bell helmets worked but a deep-skirted Icon was an interference fit. Even hanging off, the brace didn't rattle either -- nearing 170 mph on Road Aerica's front straight, it still stayed put."

"The only issue is tucking behind the fairing. Lay on the tank and the thin blade-like collar pushed up and crushes your windpipe, making it impossible to maintain a full aerodynamic tuck. This is the reason why you don't see any professional road racers rocking the Leatt yet. Speed trumps safety, at least in the pro ranks."
