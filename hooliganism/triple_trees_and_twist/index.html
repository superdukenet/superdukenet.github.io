I have had a lot of bikes in my life. They all had a central big nut that held down to top yolk or triple clamp as you call it in the USA. But underneath the yolk there was another big nut that was used to torque down onto the steering head bearings at the right force to make no play - but free turning. The top bolt - often a big shiny chrome thing - was tightened hard down as a kind of lock nut.

On the SD there isn't a nut under the top triple clamp. The big nut you see is also the one that is used to create the right force down onto the head bearings. So it is never done up really, really tight, just the right amount to ensure no play. I've never understood how much this torque should be - I can't find record of what it is meant to be and even if I did I not do I have a torque wrench socket big enough for this nut. I do it by feel with the forks and front wheel off the ground, my eyes shut and fingertip senses rocking the forks back and forth. Free movement on turn, no rocking back and forth. Job done. Then do up all the other clamp nuts. If anyone has a better way to do this then let me know because I want to know!!

So anyway, with this knowledge I would say that the big nut in the middle of your triple clamp will not be in any way responsible for stopping your forks realigning to center if the other clamp bolts on both upper and lower clamps are loosened off. It shouldn't be done up tight enough to do this. If your yolk do not center when everything is loosened off, including the front axle, then maybe you have some crash damage to the yolks and need to replace them. This is not so expensive if you keep an eye out on ebay or the forum.

PS.

I just thought of something. I crash my bike a lot at tracks. Every time the bike is OK, front end is immensely strong. But what ALWAYS needs replacing is the bolts that hold the handle bar clamps to the triple clamp. These are the weak point for the whole handlebar, fork, front wheel crash thing. The standard tapered bars are really strong and so these bolts get bent when the bike is low-sided. It's a good thing - they are cheap and easy to replace. Check them out first, if they are even the slightest bit bent then the bike will track true as you say, the forks will look straight and be visually parallel but the bars will feel like they are not 90 degrees true. This can make you think the top clamp is not true but it's an optical illusion.
