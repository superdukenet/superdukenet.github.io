2011-04-16 21:56:00 UTC
The ethanol does not technically cause this, but it is the catalyst that allows it to happen. The tanks are made of a specific nylon compound that reacts poorly to constant exposure to water. The ethanol in our fuels will wick the water particles from the fuel itself, when allowed to sit stagnant, and allows separate layers to develop in the tank: one petrol, one ethanol and one water. Over time the nylon that the tank is constructed from will absorb the water, and this is what causes the tanks to swell.

A friend of mine is an attorney in the DC area, and has been in constant communication with Ducati about this very problem, which has plagued several models over the last several years. He was actually making pretty good headway in getting a working solution developed (i.e. not going after any damages), but then a separate law firm out of San Francisco filed a class action suite against Ducati. This suite basically caused both Ducati NA and Ducati Spa to quit talking with Izaak.

Anyhow, you can read up on all the research here: http://www.ducatimonsterforum.org/index ... ic=43639.0

That's pretty ugly, but aren't these tanks made of polyethylene? That's the same stuff fuel jugs (carboys) and "gas cans" (non-metallic) are made of.

If so, it isn't ethanol that's the problem ... Ethanol is often stored and shipped (by chemical manufacturers) in polyethylene containers.

In addition to UV, polyethylene can be susceptible (but usually at elevated temperatures) to some chlorinated solvents and aromatic hydrocarbons. Its the latter of these are present in gasoline (e.g. xylene and toluene). Because polyethylene is effectively crystalline at room temperatures, it generally holds up well to the low concentrations of these chemicals in gasoline. While polyethylene has a somewhat low melting point, our gas tanks shouldn't be seeing anywhere near the needed amount of heat (temperature) to adversely affect the tank material.

I do wonder however, about whatever activator or modifier that would have to be used to get the paint to adhere to them (quite possibly these same aromatic hydrocarbons). Perhaps its a copolymerized polyethylene ... the vinyl acetates and acrylic copolymers used for this, could result in substantially increased susceptibility. Maybe the tank isn't polyethylene at all?

Regarding Ducati - isn't there a pending class action suit over their tanks (which I thought were made of polyamide - not polyethylene)? I thought I read somewhere that Acerbis makes their (and some KTM) tanks - ?

Gents - where possible, I'd be exercising those warranties. I have to wonder in any case, why some tanks exhibit this and some not.
