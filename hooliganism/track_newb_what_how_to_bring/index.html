spare pegs and lever in case you crash. Pop up canopy to stay out of the sun. Food and plenty of water. Chairs, fan for yourself and helmet depending on how hot it is. Extra fuel unless there is pumps on site.

If you ride there, be prepared to walk home. It is very likely that you will crash and injure yourself or your bike to the point where you cant/don't want to ride. Always go with friends or someone you trust to pack up your stuff for you if you have to go to the hospital.

My first year, I used a 4dr sedan and the folding trailer from Harbor Freight to get to the track. You dont have to go all out on a Toy Hauler to get there, just get a trailer or a pickup truck.

Take good food and drink. Being hungry and thirsty cos trackside food is crap sucks.

Other than that you just need a well serviced bike, with correct tyre pressures, plenty of pad life and correct chain adjustment, ...and check oil levels before you go, as topping-up at the track may not be an option if you can't find your regular make.

Riding to the track (and riding home) is a good way to calibrate your brain into sensible(ish) behaviour.
Ideally track days should offer the safest possible environemnt to ride your bike fast and hard (lots of run-off, good tarmac, no oncoming traffic, police etc) but there's not reason to ride beyondn your limits and crash just because other people are watching. ... Unless you want to start racing or are a lunatic.

I load a trailer to get my bike to the track. I'm usually pretty tired by the time I come home, and I'd ride too fast for the street if I rode my bike home. And that way I can take more stuff.

Make sure to check the requirements of your track day organization on bike prep, and check whether the track has any additional requirements. I prep my bike before I go so that I'm not rushed when I get there to pass tech.

I take all kinds of crap but actually need very little of it. Things I always use: cooler with drinks and food, chair, folding table (if I bring one), easy up if it's sunny (or find shade if I don't bring one), tyre pressure gauge, bike air pump, things I need for bike prep, tools for basic bike service, all my riding gear, fresh base layer to change if it's hot. Things I often bring but rarely use: every kind of tool I can imagine, spare parts, bike fluids and chemicals, owners/service manual. Search for checklist threads on a few track day organization forums and you'll find lots of lists of stuff to bring - most of which you'll never need!

Just a few thoughts FWIW. Have fun - you're going to love it.

Colonel_Klinck

2011-02-01 21:16:00 UTC
a ha. Reading through these answers to your question PBRnr makes me realise there are two trains of thought here. The first is what you should bring/prepare for. That's easily answered and I'm sure there will be much advice. Personally I think for your first few track days you should not get occupied with all the kit, tyre warmers, vans, track day accessories etc. Just get down there making sure that your bike is in good condition, tyres good, brake and other fluids checked, preferably an oil change - the regular pre-check routine.

The biggest issue you will face is your state of mind. Seriously, you will have butterflies the night before and will not sleep well. You need to arrive in plenty of time, give yourself a nice talk to to calm your nerves and set off determined to take it easy and be prepared to learn from scratch. Enter into the novice group with the confidence that you can ride your bike and don't get hung up on becoming a track day god on the first time out. You won't be. Just accept that you have a learning curve to go on. Don't be intimidated by all the other guys who have a lot of experience, don't envy their inters. and fast group wrist bands - you might be there one day but not today. Take it real easy, try and learn the track in the morning. Let everyone else pass you - it doesn't mean you are a wimp, it means you have a good understanding of your abilities. If you stay on and get to ride the next session - that's when you are doing well and are the hero of the day.

The "red mist". You might find that you speed up and start getting on a roll and are riding faster than you want. It sucks you in - believe me. That's when novices crash. They start to believe they are gods and start to ride at a pace they would not usually be comfortable doing. It's an ego thing. Don't let anyone tell you different, we've all done it to some extent. It is a horrible moment to suddenly realise you are crashing/running out of track space/entering a corner too fast and panicking/freezing. It happens in slow motion and you will relive it hundreds of times in the future as you replay your stupidity.

My advice is to really get your mind focussed on what your abilities are. Don't try and prove anything to anyone, except yourself. The feeling of improving and slowly getting to grips with track day riding is unbeatable when riding home after eight sessions round a great track is absolutely unforgettable and rewarding beyond your wildest dreams. But to go beyond your abilities on your first day, crashed bike, repair bills, maybe injuries leaves you feeling like a fool and maybe would even stop you having another go.

So go for it!

PS. I made ALL of the above mistakes. ALL OF THEM

So take my advice. I now ride in inters and sometimes fast. But the memories of my mistakes still haunt me, I learned the hard way.

Good luck.

I rode to the track on my first track day. I never even considered the possibility of crashing. Thankfully, I didn't crash.

I've hauled the bike for each successive track day. Its less stressful. All the track day sponsors require a certain amount of bike prep: tape lights, tape or remove mirrors, remove fuses, etc. When you haul, you can do this stuff beforehand at that makes one less thing you have to do at the track. You can concentrate on the things like finding a place to camp out, registering, tech inspection, etc. Most of all, you'll have plenty of time to make sure you make the rider's orientation meeting. If you miss that, you're out.

The other advice I have is to take plenty of fluids. You need to stay sharp. It sounds stupid, but quit when you get tired. I personally don't ride the last session. I'm just too beat by then.

tyres: My first track experience was with Reg Pridmore and his CLASS school. Reg pretty much requires new tyres. With new tyres, I'm generally moving pretty good by the end of the second lap. By session 2, the tyres are smoking - literally.

I thought requiring new tyres was a bit anal, until my last track day at Fontana. It was a bit cold, and it had rained the night before. The track didn't look wet, but after three laps, I figured my tyres would be fine, so I uncorked it going down the front straight. When I hit the brakes the bike just started to slide, so I did my best to get it pointed in the right direction and held on for dear life as i ran over the two curbs at the end of the front straight. I kept the bike up, but probably wouldn't have had the problem at all on fresh tyres. The tyres I had on the bike were baked from my last trip to the track. So, consider fresh tyres. You don't need warmers with street tyres, but they definitely work better when fresh.

Perhaps the best advise I could give you is not my advice at all. Its from Jason Pridmore. Remember, this is not a race. There are no trophies or prize money. You are there to have fun and to improve your riding skills.

WOW! Lots of posts on this one.
I will make it simple since he is talking about my home track.
PBRnr: PM me and I will fill you in or come by my house and I will SHOW you what I take.

For the benefit of others here:
Most of our track day providers call your day "over" if you crash. Even a gentle lowside at 15 mph first thing in the morning. So spare are not needed in case of a crash.
Due to the climate here I am wearing a sweatshirt in the morning and wish I was naked by mid afternoon sometimes so,
Long sleeve "Cold Gear" Under Armor for the mornings and the "Heat Gear" stuff for the afternoon.
Most of our tracks have fuel so no need to bring more than 5 gallons. The biggest deal is water and snacks. Including Pedialyte or Gatorade. There is food available at concession stands but you will want to have snacks for between rounds to keep the calories up.
Newbies will soon find that track riding at any pace is NOTHING like street riding. It is quite strenuous.

The fresh oil change is good advice but don't do it the night before unless you are a person who has high confidence that you NEVER forget a step.
A track day at Infineon is $300. Dumping oil all over the track because you forgot to torque the drain bolt or tighten the filter will make you [i]very very unpopular![i]

I especially like the advice about keeping your head. Very, very easy to get sucked in. Ride around and have a good time. Speed comes naturally with time.
