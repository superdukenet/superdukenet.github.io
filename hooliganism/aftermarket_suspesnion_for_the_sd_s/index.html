Thanks for your enquiry, we can help you out with the problem on your forks

and start again with our internals.

The internals we supply is our own GP20 cartridge style damping system. The
cartridge system is the same type of internal as what you would find in any
modern front fork. It has been manufactured and developed by our own
engineers and has pistons and shim stacks for the rebound and compression
damping. The Cartridge also has needle valves so you can adjust the
damping. The damping adjusters are external, so you can change the damping
without having to strip the forks down, but we hide the adjusters to keep
the classic appearance. As part of the conversion we also machine new fork
tops to take the new cartridges and damping adjusters. We also fit new
springs.

Every Maxton GP20 cartridge is built to order, the reason for this is to
valve and spring the cartridges to suit the fork, the bike, the riders
weight and what they are using the bike for. Once the cartridge has been
manufactured we then fit the cartridge to the fork to make sure it works
correctly. In most conversions we have to modify the original bottom
sliders and stanchions so our cartridge will fit and work properly. The
forks are then returned to you fully serviced with new seals and the spring
preload and damping set up.

The Maxton GP20 cartridge costs between £315.00 and £615.00 + V.A.T.
Depending on how adjustable you want the cartridge to be.

- For £315.00 + V.A.T the cartridge is adjustable for rebound damping.
- For £415.00 + V.A.T the cartridge is adjustable for rebound damping and
preload.
- For £515.00 + V.A.T the cartridge is adjustable for rebound and
compression damping.
- For £615.00 + V.A.T the cartridge is adjustable for rebound and
compression damping and preload.

To service the forks with new seals and install the cartridges costs an
extra £125.00 + V.A.T on top of the cartridge.

All adjustments can be made without having to strip the forks down.

LOL - reads like a canned e-mail sent to someone with a damper-rod front end, getting a cartridge re-do. I hope you don't take this this wrong way, but if you don't recognize that - then you're going to get ripped off.

Just like Maxton's stuff, The WP forks have pistons and shim stacks for the rebound and compression damping. The WP forks also have needle valves so you can adjust the damping. They are external too - not hidden. You can make all adjustments without having to strip down anything - lol.

A grand (US) is a lot of scratch for all the features the WP forks already have. Even a respring+revalve+the gratuitous proprietary piston is less from pretty much anyone who offers them. And the all the folks who offer their own pistons - think theirs is simply "the shit".

Seriously, those are great for putting on a Guzzi, ZRX, SV, Bonnie/Thruxton or whatever other decent bike still has damper rod front end or non-rebuildable cartridges. Sounds just like the same 20 mm cartridge product Traxxion offers.

Again, a lot of what suspension houses sell you as proprietary products - particularly the fork products - are to sell you their widgets. They are profitable. And they enable a big house to homogenize their approach to setup somewhat independent of the bike involved. I could go on and on about how you already paid once for all these parts, and why their products won't necessarily be any better than a decent re-spring and re-valve done by an honest and competent suspension house, who hasn't a product line to sell you.

I will say (again) that you have fine, fully adjustable, fully rebuild-able forks. Find an independent, well-reputed shop that's done a bunch of SuperDukes already, You'll save a bundle AND be happy with the outcome. Its your money ...

Viking, I have a simple solution for you, reduce your front sag to 35mm and take the forks the flush in the triples, bringing you into a more ideal sag number and giving a propper clamping area pressure for the triples all while keeping your current geometry.

But you are correct with the rear.. 35Mm works well. But if your bike is basically a streetable track bike... I would take your bike to close to zero static sag and rider sag at 30mm front and rear... Which is the amount that a few of the SD battle bikes were running. They seemed to do ok racing without any special triples or ride height adjusters.

I'm not knocking you for doing what you have done as I understand what you are trying to achieve, however it does make me nervous seeing it while keeping in mind how much you like wheelies.
