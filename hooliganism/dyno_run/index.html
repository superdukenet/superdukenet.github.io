Ducati Pete

2013-05-04 10:37:00 UTC
The correction factor used by the dyno makes a huge difference. The correction is applied to take into account changes in temperature, barometric pressure, humidity, and altitude.

It's well known that a SAE corrected dyno reads lower than one using a DIN standard. With a superduke you could easily see an additional 10 to 15 ponies just by changing between dyno types.

It's a bit of a muchness as so many other factors can influence the figure. You could ratchet up the holding straps between runs and cause slight differences.

Really, you want a base power run done on that dyno before you make any changes.

Back in the day a friend of mine opened his workshop for a day and large group of friends from one bike dealer tripped up there for a day for a "power shootout" fun thing. I know for a fact he tweeked all the results to avoid dissappointment.

This was the same dyno we tested my friends Original batch TL1000S on. (The dyno WAS NOT tweeked for optomistic results.) I can't remember what the result was but we wanted to get another twin on there straight away because Mr Suzuki had served up a treat. Oddly enough TLs the next year never seemed to be as good.

The difference between SAE and DIN is usually around 2-5 percent from my experience. Shape of the graphs should be the same though.
More important is how much pressure is in and on the rear wheel at the dyno and temperature of the air blown on the front of the bike. These things can make up to 10hp differences.
