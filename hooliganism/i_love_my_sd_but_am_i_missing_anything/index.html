Katoom

I need to confess something here. My SD is my first ever street bike. And yes I absolutely love this bike. I've been riding street for just about a year now and I've fallen in love with it. Now I have been riding and racing off-road most of my life so I do know how to ride on two wheels...but man do I have a lot to learn when it comes to street. And I'm not young at almost 51, but I am athletic and in good shape. I can tell you all that I have absolutely fallen in love with street riding. I guess sometimes we find new passions later in life. Maybe that's a good thing as now I have more to lose so I don't do anything crazy...I try to ride safe. But the bug definitely bit me and I'm trying to gobble up every bit of information on sportbike riding that I can to gain more skills. I still suck pretty badly but I am getting a little better each time I ride. The question I have may have been asked before here but I just have to ask all of you who have the benefit of experience...am I missing anything by not trying out a Japanese sport bike? I mean I constantly see R1's and GSXR's, etc. and I always wonder if they are somehow better or more fun or easier to ride and maybe better as a first bike to learn how to corner, etc, etc.??? Don't get me wrong...the SD rocks for me but it's just one of those things I wonder about. So am I missing anything?

That’s a good question and you are always going to get KTM biased answers in this church of the SD of course!

For several decades I rode nothing but jap 4’s, mostly sports bikes, mostly on the road but with a good few track miles added into the mix. I initially found the transition to the SD quite a shock as the differences in feel are quite significant. Here is what I remember most from the transition.

Sports bike engines rev much higher, some people myself included, quite like the wail of a large capacity four being tortured but that’s a side issue. What it gives you is the ability to hold gears better while accelerating and cranked over, less need to change mid corner and less chance of bumping into the limiter.

By the nature of the design they are also smoother both in general and especially in the on / off throttle transition. Finally, and if I’m honest the thing I miss most, they are much faster! Crack the throttle in third on the last gixer I had and the world went into immediate fast forward as the bike exploded towards the horizon. The people who say it’s irrelevant for road riding are of course right but it is intoxicating, my bike did better than 180MPH in 5th and I visited that area more often than I should have. That’s why I changed in the end. These sports bikes are just incredibly dull to ride under 100MPH and if you have limited self control that adds up to a ticking clock with the alarm set but hidden from view.

The riding position is much less comfortable than the SD, especially over distance. It’s not as excruciating as people make out, I toured Europe on many different sports bikes without problem, but the older you get the harder it is to accommodate.

Handling is an interesting issue. At the outer limits a modern jap sports bike will probably out corner the SD but it takes a better rider than me to explore that region. What is significantly different is the feel and position. After so long on sports bikes the sit up and beg SD took some considerable adjustment and I didn’t like it much to start with. Now I have transitioned I love the handling which I think is at least as good and possible even better for the road than any of the sports bikes I have owned. In fact on the tighter twisty roads I think the SD is probably fasted and certainly more fun!
Brakes are great on both.

A personal view, the SD is close to perfect for the sports biased road rider. If curious you should certainly try other types of road bike, you may even switch to these for a while but it would be no great surprise if the novelty wears thin after a while and you find yourself nestling back here in SD land.
Have fun.
