ShadeTheChangingMan

2011-01-15 00:30:00 UTC
This post in no way suggests that you are at the wrong end of the brake problem. It is only posted to have you take a look at the other side. When you clean the pistons and sleeves out you invariably push the piston back into it's sleeve. I believe that this amount of back pressure is enough to force the back check valve in the master cylinder, but does not alleviate the back check from being still a problem. There is a small orifice in the master that when the brakes are released it opens this orifice and let's the built up fluid pressure back into the reservoir. This has been a bone of contention on many a motorcycle brake systems. If you are experiencing continuous failure from pressure build up, I would take a good look up top and do a master rebuild.

Follow on from last nights post.

As I said I brought the bike back to the dealer this morning and I could hear the tell-tale sound of pads on discs all the way.
The dealer took the calipers off and cleared all the curd and crap and replaced the corroded pads with new ones (all free of charge)
He asked me to come back in a month time so he can check them over again.

The dealer explained to me that the problem (IHO) was that these calipers have 4 lose pads are very tightly fitted into the calipers. As dirt and road crap gets into the calipers and builds up it eventually stops the pads from returning to the original position. Friction & heat do the rest.

As he is a former KTM dealer he still has his contacts and told me he will get on to KTM to riase their awareness.
He did say that KTM are actually quite good when it comes to dealing with service issues. Their testing is extensive but do not take into account the UK & irish road conditions.

He is now the main Honda dealer and said that he is more shocked by the electical/wiring issues he comes across on the likes of VFR's and Pan Euro's. KTM has only started producing road bikes since 2005 and still have a learning curve but are responding well to issues. Honda and the likes have a few decades more experience.

So it seems the key is to keep the calipers under check and ensure that they are cleaned on a regular basis.

Colonel_Klinck

2011-01-19 08:32:00 UTC
My bad, I should have made it clearer. I removed the calliper from the fork before zip tying the brake lever. I make sure I have pumped enough brake fluid through the system to push the pistons almost all the way out on that calliper (keeping reservoir topped up with new fluid). This makes removing the pistons from the calliper a lot easier. Make sure you have both callipers to this point before removing a calliper from the line, as you won't be pushing the pistons out without the hydraulic pressure. I think some people use an air line but I don't have one so that wasn't an option.
As there is nothing stopping the pistons moving out there is no pressure, so no need to open the bleed valve before removing the brake line. Also as the brake lines internal diameter is so narrow the brake fluid holds itself in there line which again makes bleeding a lot easier.
Once the calliper is stripped and cleaned (don't loose the tiny o-ring that sits between the two halves) I put a few drops of fluid in each pot before I reinsert the pistons. Just helps prime the calliper and again makes bleeding a lot easier.
