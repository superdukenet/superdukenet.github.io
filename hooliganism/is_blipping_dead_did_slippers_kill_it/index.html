Is Blipping Dead & Did Slippers Kill It?

shadowman

2011-04-18 14:52:00 UTC

Now don’t get me wrong, I’m no luddite and in general I’m all in favour of performance enhancement through technical improvement and innovation but I struggle with the idea of a slipper clutch in the SD for the road.

It is true that for ultimate pace on the track a slipper clutch can be a great advantage. I had one on the K5 and appreciated its contribution to shaving lap time in a ‘one less thing to worry about, carefree’ sort of way. I used that bike on the road as well but never once used the slipper in anger whilst road riding.

Was I going too slow? No, I just enjoy blipping the throttle on down changes as part of the hard won muscle memory type pleasure of riding. Some well executed blips matching engine speed with road speed whilst braking hard and changing down always registered at least a flicker on the pleasurable sensation monitor.

All the more so with the SD. A big, high inertia twin with a barking exhaust and slick gearbox just begs for big blips on the way down and, because the penalty for getting it wrong is larger, in my experience the pleasure when getting it right is also magnified.

The SD is a hooligan bike, it’s a blade but not a scalpel (more of an axe if you ask me) so why would anybody want to add a slipper clutch unless they were chasing ultimate lap time? We apparently don’t want ABS (another system to mitigate incompetent rider inputs) or traction control but slipper clutches are somehow different.

Is it just me that enjoys blipping on down changes as part of the rider skill set, is it a dead or dying art?

Riding, and especially on something like the SD, is surely not about convenience or safety. It's about man & machine, about control and exhilaration. Simplicity rules with mechanical (or other) interventions kept to a minimum.

What do you think, to blip or not to blip and slip......

Dutch

2011-04-18 16:31:00 UTC

I had an SD without a slipper, and then bought one, because I was doing a lot of track days, and if you dive into the hairpin at Brands on full chat you find yourslef spending more time thinking about gear choice and clucth use than you do about lines and the people around you... so a slipper seemed like a good idea.

The minute it was fitted I forgot about it, as it's action is invisible (although you do feel it through the clucth lever) - but on my first day back at Brands, I found the hairpin a lot easier to get around, and realised that I was thinking more about my bike's route through each corner and less about my bike's mechanics or gear selection.

And regarding your comments about laptimes, etc, it's not about that at all, it's about enjoying the bike's handling more and thinking less about the inherant problems of big CC twins with gears.

If you ride hard or do track days often it's a great mod, but I do also get it working on the road, and with one less thing to think about I think it makes for a safer and more rewarding ride... ...but they're not cheap and of course you can ride hard without one.

I'm an old time biker and I love my blipping. These V twin engines sound lovely when you blip. I love the matching of revs to speed to gears to road. Like been stated it's part of the riding experience.

But i have a slipper fitted and it's a better bike. I still blip on most of the road tiding I do but on those occasions where I go down to fast or misjudge entry speed then it's a godsend. I don't just whack down through the box and dump the clutch but when you do it makes things OK. But I'm not a racer. On track I know that when I'm riding as fast as I can then like Dutch says it's one less thing to worry about and I can concentrate on more important things like apexes and tyre grip.

Same with a quickshifter by the way. On the road I still use the clutch most of the time. It only works on hard acceleration really - the more you pin it the more it makes sense - not something you do much in town ever. In fact when I miss it and try to use it in town you realise just how much faster you have to ride and then you're going too fast for the situation usually.
