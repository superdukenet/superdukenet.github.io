Noob question about fuel injection

Scribe78

2011-11-01 03:00:00 UTC
Just got my newly acquired '07 up into the mountains for the first time and found it to be much more intimidating in the very tight stuff than I expected. Specifically, the transitions between on- and off-throttle were so abrupt and dramatic that I had trouble maintaining my concentration on other factors, like line selection. Everywhere else I rode this bike, I loved the hard-hitting low-end grunt and I've always appreciated the engine-braking on twins. I've had lots of other sport bikes, but only one other with fuel injection - a Suzuki TL1000S a little over 10 years ago. That bike had a similar problem, and it was enough to ruin my fun (I wound up fleeing to a CBR600F4, but then missed the big-twin experience so much I came back to a SuperHawk).

My SD's previous owner installed FMF Apex slip-ons, MH airbox (SAS and O2 sensors removed) and a PCIII (with custom mapping), along with a softer sloping throttle cam. As far as I could tell before this last outing, fuel metering was very good all around. I didn't detect any of the low-rpm surging or other misbehavior I'd read about on stock SD's. It wasn't until I was dealing with on/off throttle transitions during multiple series of tight corners that I had any complaints, but then it was really bad. Is this something that can be addressed with further modification? Does this indicate the need for better mapping? Would the MH secondary butterflies help? Do I need a slipper clutch? Obviously, I'll be practicing my throttle control to minimize my own contribution to the situation, too.

On the positive side, the SD's handling was magnificent -- as you all know already!

Thanks in advance for your input.

I maybe reaching here - but I'm gonna guess that pretty much everyone on here has had if not the same, nearly the same experiences. I'm guessing, that with that set of mods, the bike has already been re-mapped. With TuneECU (free, but please donate!) - you can determine this.

Otherwise, what you describe is part of the charm. Japanese I4 600s they ain't. Not even close. That low-end instant-on torque punch is part of what makes the bike so much fun. Bike wants to lift out of every corner effortlessly - tee-hee.

OK - while these things are an acquired taste (read: not for everyone), use a lower gear(s) maybe; keep the revs up. After the apex - bring in as much throttle as quickly as you dare. Word of caution: Extreme euphoric giddiness can occur, followed by desire to repeat the experience as many times as possible. Not unlike your 'ol TL (desmoquattro, etc.), if you ride it like it was stolen, it seems to work better.

One thing I forgot to mention is if you have tuneecu make sure to balance throttle bodies. What I do to set them is start engine let it get hot then hook up Tuneecu.
Now I don't set them at idle I actually put a piece of model engine fuel line in between the throttle grip and switch housing so I can keep the throttle just open from fully closed. The model engine fuel line acts like a throttle lock! At Idle the reading will be some were around the 380s to 390s on the front and rear cylinder or more. Then what I do is raise the throttle till I get a reading around 400 to 405 now because you have the throttle locked with the model engine fuel line you now have both hands free to do your adjustments. When I do mine I put the throttle up so I have a reading of 401 and then adjusted the other cylinder to the best I could get. It was switching from 401 to 402 on the other cylinder so I was happy with that. Once you are happy with setting give it a rev and then check again. Have a look at this also it shows you the adjustment screw and another method of adjustment if you don't have tuneecu. viewtopic.php?t=5347

people think removing the secondaries is some kind of miracle cure. its not. you have to learn to ride this bike. its a big twin and has a huge amount of engine braking, keep the throttle cracked always in a corner.
if you just remove the secondaries and not understand the power delivery you still arent gonna like it (secondaries have nothing to do with engine braking). if you didnt like the TL you probably arent going to come to terms with this one which is a shame. give it some time and learn the bike before you slam it into the turns for your own safety!

what does in fact help with the abruptness is keeping a couple of fingers on the brake lever to have a common reference, it smooths out the throttle movement which can throw anyone by hitting a bump which will telescope any movement directly to the throttle. also relax your arms, if they are rigid youre gonna have the same problem, try to relax and let the bike do what its gonna do. ive been riding for 30 years and these 2 things made the bike much more fun, after a while (unless your tired and forget...) youll adapt and love the thing.
