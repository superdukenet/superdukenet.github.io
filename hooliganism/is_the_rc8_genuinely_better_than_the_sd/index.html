2011-09-01 15:35:00 UTC
I had my GSXR 750 before buying my SD. I racked up quite a few miles on it road and track. I installed Helibars on it early on and at my height of riding it, could do 400 mile days (slab and twisties) without feeling it the next day.

Before buying my SD, I test rode an RC8 (not R). I'd read all the hype about what a comfortable RR it was. I call BS. After 20 min, I wanted my GSXR back. It might be heresy to say on this site, but the GSXR 750 does everything better than the standard RC8. The GSXR is faster, handles better, and rides better (street or track) and doesn't cook your legs in traffic like the RC8. Dump the extra money you'll spend on the RC8 on Ohlins 30mm fork cartridges and a TTX shock and OMFG - the bike is a weapon.

So why the eff am I on the SD.net board spouting the virtues of a GSXR 750? Because the SD is by FAR a better street bike IMO. The riding position is more comfortable than any SS - even my GSXR with Helibars. Its easier to maneuver in the city and while splitting traffic. It's not as top heavy as a SS so its easier to ride at parking lot speeds. (It doesn't want to suddenly take a nap on its side as you roll up to the gas pump. During my test ride, the guy in front of me dumped the other RC8 test bike in the parking lot. ) It's far more nimble. You can put it anywhere in the lane and carve through or adjust mid corner without major issues. Yes, fighting the wind blast on the freeway isn't fun, but that is the big trade off. The other trade off is that it won't hit 170 mph.

If I want to go fast (>120 mph) I'll grab the GSXR. Its easier to deal with at speed. (The fairings make it easier to move around on the bike at speed.) The GSXR has become my track bike. But, for most road work, the SD gets the nod. I suspect that if you had the two, you'd gravitate toward the same conclusion. Flame on. (Its OK. I have my Nomex suit handy).

Edit: FWIW, I don't think either bike is the perfect compromise. I'm still looking for that bike. Let me know if you find it.

This is my personal philosophy on the different types of bikes and what they should be used for. I was talking bikes with a friend of mine and he said he wanted to get his first bike soon. I asked what kind he was looking at and he replied "probably an R6". I asked if he was ever going to track the bike and he said "no I just want a commuter". So I asked him:

"Would you ever take a Harley or a cruiser to the track? No, that would be dumb right? So why would you ride a race replica bike on the street?"

As far as the street is concerned I'll only ever ride supermoto's/street fighters. I think it's a little silly that some people ride super sports as a method of point A to point B. Can it be done? Yes. Do some enjoy it? I'm sure. But riding a super sport on the street is like having a lion as a house cat. It's possible but the bike will be almost completely out of it's element. As sexy as the RC8 is, I wouldn't touch one unless I knew I was going to track it for a good chunk of it's life, maybe ride it on the street every now and again. My thoughts for what they are worth.

My last bike was a gixer 1000 K5. This had just over 160 RWHP, weighed less than an SD, great brakes, handled like a dream, was not uncomfortable (I rode 400+ miles in a day several times without problems) and being two tone black it was also beautiful to look at. Sounds like I wanted to marry it doesn't it

Thing is that in the end I hardly rode it on the road at all because it was just astonishingly boring below three figure speeds and with my low boredom threshold if I did take it out I would be in 'go to prison' territory almost immediately I left the village. Yes it kept me comfortably in the fast group at tracks compensating for my modest talent with brute force and composure but I was actually beaten round Silverstone GP (one of the fastest and most open circuits in the world) by Dave Woods on a Superduke. Proof if any were needed that at our level it's talent and not equipment that makes the difference.

I did consider the RC8 when I switched from the K5 but in the end I would have had the same issues on a more expensive and (slightly) less capable machine so I couldn't see the point and went for the SD instead.

I bought the SD to slow down and have more fun on the roads and now that I have got used to it I enjoy myself more and ride more often as a result. I have no idea how well I will go with it on track but if it's dry I'm going to wobble round Donington on the 8th and find out.

So to address your question directly the answer is both yes and no. Yes the K5 was better in every measurable way to the SD (power, speed etc etc) as the RC8 is to the SD but no as in reality these positives make the road riding experience worse in many ways and the cheeky fun of the naked SD more than compensates for any losses in other areas.

If you need weather and wind protection for work travel why not get a cheap car instead and save the two wheel stuff for amusement only?

Is it better? Depends on what you want to do with it.

I had the opportunity to attend a KTM Ride_Orange track day last week, at Mid-Ohio. They had both the RC8 and SMR available to ride, Being my first track day, I opted to ride the SMR the first 20 minute session. It was a good way to familarize myself with the track and the mannerisms associated with this type of event. Mind you, I was in the novice class, as to be expected. The second session I went out on the RC8. Very foreign compared to the SMR and my SD. By the 3rd lap I started to gel with the RC8 but it still wasn't as fun as the SMR. My wrist were sore and my back didn't feel good after I got off the bike.
For the rest of the day (six sessions total) I rode the SMR. My lap times increased and I was holding my own against the RC8 riders in my group in the tight stuff. It came down to how much fun was I going to have on which bike. I am sure after a full day on the RC8 I would have liked it more than that second session. But the fun I was having riding the SMR and the stupid grin I couldn't remove from my face, after what seemed to me as some hot laps, was what I was there for. Regardless, the whole track day experience is something I am going to do again. How can you go wrong with new KTM's, fuel, several mechanics, fairly new tyres and unlimited instruction from some very qualified riders.
To me the RC8 is a purpose built track bike. If that's what you're after, you will not be disappointed. I'll stick with my SD but preferably an SMR.
