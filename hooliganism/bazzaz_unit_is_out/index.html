The Bazzaz TC is "real". Its not imaginary, or work by some form of magic. It really does detect wheelspin, and intervene to aid in maintaining traction.

Yes, it monitors the rate of change of engine (and therefore wheel) speed as a function of throttle input, etc - which is fully a metric by which the degree of wheelspin (loss of traction) can be defined. It uses no additional sensors to do the sensing. Some other systems use wheel rings (like those used with ABS systems, or on some systems - the same rings), with and without the bike's pitch and roll consideration (tilt sensors and/or accelerometers). The wheel sensor type measures not only wheel and engine rate of change relative to control inputs, but also relative to the bike's speed (like ABS sensors).

The nice thing about the Bazzaz, is that its pretty much a plug-and-play unit which also has all the mapping capability of PCIII/V, and an integral quickshifter capability which allows full throttle clutchless upshifts. User can set the amount of wheelspin allowed (optional h'bar switch is available). An optional autotuning module eliminates the need for any dyno time for optimum mapping (yes, O2 sensor based ...).

How the "control" part works (oversimplified), when wheelspin (traction loss) is detected (by whatever scheme), power is progressively cut - regardless of throttle input, to end wheelspin (restoring traction, and likely sliding). Its done in a manner to least upset the chassis, then power is gradually fed back in as traction is restored. All this can happen before the rider can drop the throttle. There are varying schemes in the power reduction including reduced fuel delivery to one or more cylinders, timing retardation or a combination of that.

The Bazzaz is a tempting product, but I don't know how useful it is for the expert-level rider on a bike that make only a little more than 100HP (SD) - even on the track. Don't get me wrong, I've spun up 70HP bikes where the bike stepped out so bad, it was amazing I didn't highside a few times - but I didn't just drop the throttle when it happened. For some, it might be a real bacon saver.

On an RC8R, until KTM comes up with their own TC, I'd pop for a Bazzaz in a heartbeat.

I believe all systems would have to use a mathematical algorithm to determine loss of traction, I would have thought wheel speed vs the engine speed would be an efficent way to do this, I imagine anti wheelie systems monitor the front wheel and TC the rear.

I do think it's probably a bit excessive for a relatively low powered SD though but having not ridden off road I couldn't say whether it is really a good thing to have in those conditions on an adventure, I'd have thought you might want to keep the revs up and power through not have them cut if on a mixture of surfaces but as I say, I have no experience in that, so I know nothing about what's required, it's just the thoughts that came into my head, at least I've never heard Sabre or anyone say cut your throttle to get traction, it's usually pin it to win it.

However, if you like to ride on track then in my mind traction control is just taking away from your own development as a rider, if my lap times were to improve and could suddenly get past my friends just because I could open the throttle earlier without fear of losing it then it wouldn't really be an achievement, it would be a false victory, if I learn how early and how much power I can lay down on my own then that is something real to be proud of.

We're not MotoGP riders riding for factories who need to put everything they can into a bike to be the fastest bike on track regardless, our careers don't depend on our results, we're all doing it for ourselves to ride our bikes to the best of our abilities.

I never really followed racing that intensely but one thing that sticks in my mind about the old school top racers was the mention of their amazing throttle control and how races could be won or lost as a result of throttle control alone.

It's potentially a nice safety net for road riding on the crappy uk roads if that's a concern but for that alone it's not something I'd be rushing out to spend money on.

It would be a laugh to try it out of course
